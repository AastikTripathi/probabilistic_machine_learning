    Chapter 67: Generative Adversarial Networks

    67.1 Recapitulation and Roadmap

    In the previous chapter we established the master framework of generative modeling. Every generative model is, at bottom, a solution to the following problem:

    \[\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \, D(p \,\|\, p_\theta), \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{x}} = G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z}), \quad \mathbf{z} \sim p_Z \tag{67.1}\]

    where \(p\) is the unknown data distribution, \(p_\theta\) is the push-forward of a known noise distribution \(p_Z\) through a parametric function \(G_\theta\), and \(D\) is some divergence measure.

    Three questions govern every specific model: 1. What is \(p_Z\)? 2. What is \(G_\theta\)? 3. What is \(D\), and how is \(\min_\theta D(p \| p_\theta)\) solved given that neither \(p\) nor \(p_\theta\) is available in closed form?

    For Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), the answers are: 1. \(p_Z = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) — a \(k\)-dimensional standard normal (rather than Uniform, for reasons discussed in §67.2). 2. \(G_\theta\) is a deep neural network, typically with an expanding architecture (mapping \(\mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d\) with \(k < d\)). 3. \(D\) belongs to the family of \(f\)-divergences, specifically the Jensen-Shannon divergence. Because this divergence is intractable, we construct a variational lower bound on it; tightening that bound introduces a second neural network \(D_\phi\), the discriminator (or critic). The result is a saddle-point optimization problem over \((\theta, \phi)\).

    This chapter builds the GAN formulation from the ground up: from the choice of \(p_Z\), through the \(f\)-divergence family and its variational representation, to the minimax objective, the training algorithm, and a careful analysis of what the optimal solution means.

    67.2 Why \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\) Instead of Uniform?

    In Chapter 66 we took \(p_Z = \text{Uniform}[0,1]\) as the canonical starting point, motivated by the Probability Integral Transform. In practice, however, generative models for continuous data use \(p_Z = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\).

    The reason is support. A \(k\)-dimensional uniform distribution has support \([0,1]^k\), a bounded hypercube. Natural data distributions — images, audio, text embeddings — have unbounded support in \(\mathbb{R}^d\): any value, however extreme, occurs with some nonzero probability. If \(p_Z\) has bounded support and \(G_\theta\) is a continuous function, then by the continuous image theorem \(G_\theta([0,1]^k)\) is also bounded, and \(p_\theta\) cannot assign nonzero density to unbounded regions.

    \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) has full support on \(\mathbb{R}^k\): for every open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k\), \(p_Z(U) > 0\). Therefore, \(p_\theta\) can in principle place mass anywhere in \(\mathbb{R}^d\), matching the full support of \(p\).

    Moreover, we can sample from \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) for the same reason we can sample from Uniform\([0,1]\): there exists a known deterministic transformation from \(\text{Uniform}[0,1]^k\) to \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\), called the Box-Muller transform (one concrete instance of the Push-Forward Principle itself).

    Box-Muller Transform (Example 67.1). Given \(U_1, U_2 \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0,1]\), define:

    \[Z_1 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1} \cos(2\pi U_2), \qquad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1} \sin(2\pi U_2)\]

    Then \(Z_1, Z_2 \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). The full derivation uses the change-of-variables formula for the joint density of \((U_1, U_2)\) under the polar coordinate transformation, which the reader can verify as an exercise. The key takeaway is that sampling from \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) is itself an instance of the master framework — we will simply accept it as a primitive going forward.

    67.3 The \(f\)-Divergence Family

    The choice of divergence \(D\) in (67.1) is not arbitrary — it fundamentally shapes the geometry of the optimization landscape and the behavior of the learned model. GANs use a large and principled family called \(f\)-divergences, which contains the KL divergence, reverse KL, Jensen-Shannon, \(\chi^2\), and many others as special cases.

    67.3.1 Definition

    Definition 67.1 (\(f\)-Divergence). Let \(f : (0, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}\) be a convex, lower-semicontinuous function with \(f(1) = 0\). The \(f\)-divergence between distributions \(p\) and \(q\) (with \(p \ll q\), i.e., \(p\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(q\)) is:

    \[D_f(p \,\|\, q) = \int_{\mathcal{X}} q(\mathbf{x}) \, f\!\left(\frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})}\right) d\mathbf{x} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim q}\!\left[f\!\left(\frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})}\right)\right] \tag{67.2}\]

    The function \(f\) is called the generator function of the divergence. (Beware the overloaded terminology: in the GAN context, "generator" refers to the network \(G_\theta\); here it is a scalar function defining the divergence. These are distinct uses of the same word.)

    Properties. For any valid \(f\): - \(D_f(p \| q) \geq 0\) for all \(p, q\). - \(D_f(p \| q) = 0\) if and only if \(p = q\) (almost everywhere). - \(D_f\) is generally not symmetric: \(D_f(p \| q) \neq D_f(q \| p)\) in general.

    The non-negativity follows from Jensen's inequality: since \(f\) is convex,

    \[\mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[f\!\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)\right] \geq f\!\left(\mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[\frac{p}{q}\right]\right) = f\!\left(\int p(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x}\right) = f(1) = 0\]

    where we used \(\int p(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x} = 1\).

    67.3.2 Special Cases

    The following table shows how the standard divergences arise as special cases.

    \(f(t)\) \(D_f(p \| q)\) Name
    \(t \ln t\) \(D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = \int p \ln \frac{p}{q}\) KL divergence (forward)
    \(-\ln t\) \(D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \int q \ln \frac{q}{p}\) Reverse KL divergence
    \((t-1)^2\) \(\chi^2(p \| q) = \int \frac{(p-q)^2}{q}\) Chi-squared divergence
    \(\frac{1}{2}\left(t \ln t - (t+1)\ln\frac{t+1}{2}\right)\) \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q)\) Jensen-Shannon divergence
    \(\|t - 1\|\) $TV(p, q) = \frac{1}{2}\int p - q

    Worked Example 67.1: Verifying KL as an \(f\)-divergence.

    Take \(f(t) = t \ln t\). Then:

    \[D_f(p \| q) = \int q(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})} \ln \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})} \, d\mathbf{x} = \int p(\mathbf{x}) \ln \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})} \, d\mathbf{x} = D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) \checkmark\]

    Note \(f(1) = 1 \cdot \ln 1 = 0\) ✓ and \(f''(t) = 1/t > 0\) for \(t > 0\), so \(f\) is convex ✓.

    67.3.3 Why Not Just Use KL?

    This is a natural question. We used \(D_{\text{KL}}\) for all our discriminative models (maximum likelihood minimizes \(D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_\theta)\)), so why not here?

    The issue is the support mismatch problem. Recall that \(D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = \int p \ln \frac{p}{q}\). If there exists a region \(A\) where \(p(A) > 0\) but \(q(A) = 0\), then \(\frac{p}{q} \to \infty\) on \(A\) and \(D_{\text{KL}}(p \| q) = +\infty\). In the generative setting, early in training \(p_\theta\) is randomly initialized and its support may not overlap with \(p\) at all. This makes the KL gradient uninformative — it is \(+\infty\) everywhere that \(p_\theta\) has no support.

    Reverse KL \(D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p)\) has the opposite pathology: it is \(+\infty\) when \(q\) assigns mass where \(p\) does not, forcing the model to be zero-avoiding (it must cover everything \(p\) covers) but potentially ignoring modes of \(p\) it hasn't yet found. The two behaviors are called mass-covering (forward KL) and mode-seeking (reverse KL).

    Jensen-Shannon divergence \(D_{\text{JS}}\) is a symmetric interpolation between the two, bounded in \([0, \ln 2]\), and well-defined even when \(p\) and \(p_\theta\) have disjoint support (it evaluates to \(\ln 2\) in that case, rather than \(\infty\)). This makes it more numerically stable for early-stage training and provides gradients even when supports do not overlap. These properties motivated Goodfellow et al. (2014) to build the original GAN around \(D_{\text{JS}}\).

    67.4 The Variational Representation of \(f\)-Divergences

    The central mathematical obstacle is that we cannot compute \(D_f(p \| p_\theta)\) — we know neither \(p\) nor \(p_\theta\) in closed form. We only have samples from each.

    The key breakthrough is that every \(f\)-divergence admits a variational lower bound expressible purely in terms of expectations over samples. This is the Fenchel conjugate (or Legendre transform) representation.

    67.4.1 The Fenchel Conjugate

    Definition 67.2 (Fenchel Conjugate). For a function \(f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\), its Fenchel conjugate (convex conjugate) is:

    \[f^*(s) = \sup_{t \in \text{dom}(f)} \left\{st - f(t)\right\} \tag{67.3}\]

    For convex \(f\), the Fenchel-Moreau theorem guarantees that \(f^{**} = f\), i.e., \(f\) is recoverable from \(f^*\):

    \[f(t) = \sup_{s \in \text{dom}(f^*)} \left\{st - f^*(s)\right\} \tag{67.4}\]

    Example 67.2. For \(f(t) = t \ln t\) (KL generator):

    \[f^*(s) = \sup_t \{st - t\ln t\}\]

    Setting \(\frac{d}{dt}[st - t\ln t] = s - \ln t - 1 = 0\) gives \(t^* = e^{s-1}\), so:

    \[f^*(s) = s \cdot e^{s-1} - e^{s-1}\ln(e^{s-1}) = s e^{s-1} - (s-1)e^{s-1} = e^{s-1}\]

    Therefore \(f^*(s) = e^{s-1}\), with domain \(\mathbb{R}\).

    67.4.2 The Variational Lower Bound

    Theorem 67.1 (Variational Representation of \(f\)-Divergences, Nguyen et al. 2010). For any \(f\)-divergence:

    \[D_f(p \,\|\, q) = \sup_{T : \mathcal{X} \to \text{dom}(f^*)} \left\{ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[T(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim q}[f^*(T(\mathbf{x}))] \right\} \tag{67.5}\]

    where the supremum is over all measurable functions \(T : \mathcal{X} \to \text{dom}(f^*)\).

    Proof. Starting from (67.2) and substituting (67.4):

    \[D_f(p \| q) = \int q(\mathbf{x}) f\!\left(\frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})}\right) d\mathbf{x} = \int q(\mathbf{x}) \sup_s \left\{s \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})} - f^*(s)\right\} d\mathbf{x}\]

    Since the supremum over \(s\) can be taken pointwise and \(q > 0\):

    \[\geq \sup_T \int q(\mathbf{x}) \left\{T(\mathbf{x}) \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})} - f^*(T(\mathbf{x}))\right\} d\mathbf{x}\]
    \[= \sup_T \left\{\int p(\mathbf{x}) T(\mathbf{x}) d\mathbf{x} - \int q(\mathbf{x}) f^*(T(\mathbf{x})) d\mathbf{x}\right\}\]
    \[= \sup_T \left\{\mathbb{E}_p[T(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_q[f^*(T(\mathbf{x}))]\right\}\]

    The inequality becomes an equality when \(T^*(\mathbf{x}) = \arg\sup_s\{s \cdot p(\mathbf{x})/q(\mathbf{x}) - f^*(s)\}\), which by (67.3)–(67.4) is \(T^*(\mathbf{x}) = (f^*)^{-1}(p(\mathbf{x})/q(\mathbf{x}))\) (or equivalently, where the pointwise supremum is attained). \(\blacksquare\)

    What does this tell us? The \(f\)-divergence equals the supremum of a quantity that involves only: - An expectation over \(p\) (estimable from real data \(\mathcal{D}\)), and - An expectation over \(q = p_\theta\) (estimable by generating samples through \(G_\theta\)).

    The function \(T\) is the variational parameter. Approximating \(T\) with a neural network \(T_\phi\) — restricting the supremum to the class of functions representable by that network — gives us a computable lower bound:

    \[D_f(p \,\|\, p_\theta) \geq \sup_\phi \left\{ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[T_\phi(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[f^*(T_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))] \right\} \tag{67.6}\]

    This is precisely the bound whose tightness requires solving the inner maximization over \(\phi\). Plugging this bound back into our goal of minimizing \(D_f(p \| p_\theta)\) over \(\theta\) gives the GAN saddle-point problem:

    \[\min_\theta \max_\phi \left\{ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[T_\phi(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[f^*(T_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))] \right\} \tag{67.7}\]

    This is the general \(f\)-GAN objective (Nowozin et al., 2016). The specific functional form of the objective depends on the choice of \(f\). Let us now work out the case \(f = D_{\text{JS}}\) in detail.

    67.5 The Jensen-Shannon GAN: Full Derivation

    67.5.1 The Jensen-Shannon Divergence

    Recall from Chapter 66:

    \[D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(p \,\Big\|\, \frac{p+q}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2} D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(q \,\Big\|\, \frac{p+q}{2}\right) \tag{67.8}\]

    We can write this using the mixture distribution \(m = \frac{p+q}{2}\):

    \[D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = \frac{1}{2}\int p \ln\frac{p}{m} + \frac{1}{2}\int q \ln\frac{q}{m} = \ln 2 - H\!\left(\frac{p+q}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2}H(p) + \frac{1}{2}H(q)\]

    where \(H\) denotes differential entropy. Key properties: - \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) \in [0, \ln 2]\), bounded regardless of support overlap. - \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = 0 \Leftrightarrow p = q\). - Symmetric: \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = D_{\text{JS}}(q \| p)\). - \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = \ln 2\) when \(p\) and \(q\) have completely disjoint support.

    67.5.2 The Minimax Value Function

    In the original GAN paper, Goodfellow et al. (2014) proposed the following value function directly:

    \[V(G_\theta, D_\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[\ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x})] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))] \tag{67.9}\]

    where \(D_\phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to (0,1)\) (with a sigmoid output layer). The GAN training objective is:

    \[\min_\theta \max_\phi \, V(G_\theta, D_\phi) \tag{67.10}\]

    We will now rigorously derive that this is equivalent to minimizing \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta)\).

    67.5.3 Step 1 — Optimal Discriminator for Fixed \(G_\theta\)

    Proposition 67.1. For any fixed generator \(G_\theta\) (and thus fixed \(p_\theta\)), the discriminator that maximizes \(V(G_\theta, D_\phi)\) over all measurable functions \(D : \mathbb{R}^d \to (0,1)\) is:

    \[D^*(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})} \tag{67.11}\]

    Proof. Since the expectations in (67.9) are over the data space, we can write:

    \[V = \int_{\mathcal{X}} \left[ p(\mathbf{x}) \ln D(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \ln(1 - D(\mathbf{x})) \right] d\mathbf{x}\]

    (using \(\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[f(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))] = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta}[f(\mathbf{x})]\) by the push-forward). The integrand decomposes pointwise: for each \(\mathbf{x}\), we maximize \(h(y) = a \ln y + b \ln(1-y)\) over \(y \in (0,1)\) with \(a = p(\mathbf{x}) > 0\) and \(b = p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) > 0\).

    \[h'(y) = \frac{a}{y} - \frac{b}{1-y} = 0 \implies a(1-y) = by \implies y^* = \frac{a}{a+b} = \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}\]

    Checking the second derivative: \(h''(y) = -a/y^2 - b/(1-y)^2 < 0\), confirming this is a maximum. \(\blacksquare\)

    Interpretation. \(D^*(\mathbf{x})\) is exactly the Bayes-optimal classifier for the binary problem of distinguishing whether \(\mathbf{x}\) came from \(p\) (real, label \(1\)) or \(p_\theta\) (generated, label \(0\)). When \(p = p_\theta\), \(D^*(\mathbf{x}) = 1/2\) everywhere — the discriminator is no better than a coin flip.

    Remark. A discriminator with sigmoid output naturally parameterizes \(D_\phi(\mathbf{x}) \in (0,1)\). In the limit of infinite capacity, \(D_\phi\) can approximate any measurable function, so this optimal solution is achievable.

    67.5.4 Step 2 — Substituting \(D^*\) and Connecting to \(D_{\text{JS}}\)

    Theorem 67.2. The minimax value of the GAN game equals \(2D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta) - 2\ln 2\):

    \[\max_\phi V(G_\theta, D_\phi) = 2 D_{\text{JS}}(p \,\|\, p_\theta) - 2\ln 2 \tag{67.12}\]

    Proof. Substitute \(D^*\) from (67.11) into (67.9):

    \[V(G_\theta, D^*) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}\!\left[\ln \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}\right] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta}\!\left[\ln \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}\right]\]

    In each term, add and subtract \(\ln 2\) inside:

    \[= \mathbb{E}_{p}\!\left[\ln\frac{2 p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})} - \ln 2\right] + \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}\!\left[\ln\frac{2 p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})} - \ln 2\right]\]
    \[= \mathbb{E}_{p}\!\left[\ln\frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{m(\mathbf{x})}\right] + \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}\!\left[\ln\frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{m(\mathbf{x})}\right] - 2\ln 2\]

    where \(m(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{2}\) is the mixture. Recognizing the KL divergences:

    \[= D_{\text{KL}}(p \,\|\, m) + D_{\text{KL}}(p_\theta \,\|\, m) - 2\ln 2 = 2 D_{\text{JS}}(p \,\|\, p_\theta) - 2\ln 2 \qquad \blacksquare\]

    Corollary 67.1. The outer minimization

    \[\min_\theta \max_\phi V(G_\theta, D_\phi) = \min_\theta \left[2 D_{\text{JS}}(p \,\|\, p_\theta) - 2\ln 2\right]\]

    is equivalent to \(\min_\theta D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta)\), which is minimized (to \(0\)) if and only if \(p_\theta = p\).

    At the global optimum: - \(p_{\theta^*} = p\) (the generator perfectly matches the data distribution), - \(D^*(\mathbf{x}) = 1/2\) for all \(\mathbf{x}\) (the discriminator cannot distinguish real from generated), - \(V^* = -2\ln 2 \approx -1.386\).

    67.6 The Saddle-Point Structure and Why It Is Hard

    67.6.1 What Is a Saddle Point?

    Definition 67.3 (Saddle Point). A point \((\theta^*, \phi^*)\) is a saddle point of \(V(\theta, \phi)\) if:

    \[V(\theta^*, \phi) \leq V(\theta^*, \phi^*) \leq V(\theta, \phi^*) \quad \forall \, \theta, \phi\]

    In other words, \((\theta^*, \phi^*)\) is a minimum of \(V\) with respect to \(\theta\) and simultaneously a maximum with respect to \(\phi\).

    Geometrically, imagine the surface of \(V\) over \((\theta, \phi)\)-space. At a saddle point, slicing along the \(\theta\)-axis gives a local minimum, while slicing along the \(\phi\)-axis gives a local maximum — like the surface of a horse's saddle (hence the name). The landscape curves downward in one direction and upward in another.

    67.6.2 The Alternating Optimization Strategy

    Standard gradient descent seeks a minimum of a function by always moving downhill. For a saddle-point problem, we need to move downhill in \(\theta\) and uphill in \(\phi\) simultaneously. The standard approach is alternating gradient descent-ascent:

    For each iteration \(t\): 1. Fix \(\theta\), take a gradient ascent step in \(\phi\): \(\phi \leftarrow \phi + \eta \nabla_\phi V\). 2. Fix \(\phi\), take a gradient descent step in \(\theta\): \(\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta V\).

    This is not guaranteed to converge in general, even for convex-concave problems with non-trivial structure. In the deep neural network setting, where both \(G_\theta\) and \(D_\phi\) are non-convex, convergence guarantees are essentially absent. This fundamental difficulty is the primary reason GAN training is notoriously unstable, prone to oscillation (alternating between regions rather than converging) and mode collapse (the generator producing only a few distinct samples).

    67.6.3 Intuitive Interpretation: The Adversarial Game

    Although the saddle-point formulation is the correct mathematical view, there is a complementary intuition that has become standard in the field.

    Think of \(D_\phi\) as a detective and \(G_\theta\) as a forger. The detective's job is to inspect a piece of data \(\mathbf{x}\) and decide: is this authentic (from \(p\)) or counterfeit (from \(p_\theta\))? The forger's job is to produce counterfeits \(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})\) that are indistinguishable from authentic data.

    The loss function encodes this competition: - The discriminator maximizes: assign high \(D_\phi(\mathbf{x})\) to real data, low \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))\) to fake data. - The generator minimizes (equivalently, tries to maximize \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))\)): produce fakes that the discriminator scores highly.

    Equilibrium (saddle point) is reached when the forger is so good that the detective cannot do better than guessing (\(D^* = 1/2\) everywhere). At that point, \(p_\theta = p\) and the generative modeling problem is solved.

    A caution. This narrative, while pedagogically useful, can be misleading if taken as the starting point (rather than a consequence) of the mathematical construction. The adversarial game is not what defines GANs; it is what emerges from the variational representation of the Jensen-Shannon divergence. Starting from the game story leads to ad hoc constructions that are hard to analyze theoretically.

    67.7 Detailed Training Algorithm

    We now make the training procedure fully explicit, tracking every tensor and gradient.

    67.7.1 Network Architectures

    Generator \(G_\theta : \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d\) takes a latent vector \(\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^k\) (\(k \ll d\)) and produces a synthetic data point \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \in \mathbb{R}^d\). Because the mapping goes from lower to higher dimension, the network expands: successive layers have increasing width, typically with transposed convolutions (for image data) or dense layers followed by reshape operations.

    Discriminator \(D_\phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to (0,1)\) takes a data point \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) (either real or generated) and outputs a scalar in \((0,1)\), interpreted as the probability that \(\mathbf{x}\) came from \(p\) (real). The architecture mirrors the generator in reverse: contracting (for image data, convolutional with pooling or strided convolutions), ending with a single sigmoid unit.

    67.7.2 Mini-Batch Approximation of the Objective

    The expectations in (67.9) are approximated via mini-batches of size \(m\):

    \[\hat{V}_D = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}) + \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ln\!\left(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))\right) \tag{67.13}\]
    \[\hat{V}_G = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ln\!\left(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))\right) \tag{67.14}\]

    where \(\{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim p\) (a mini-batch from \(\mathcal{D}\)) and \(\{\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\).

    67.7.3 Gradient Computation

    Discriminator gradient. We want \(\nabla_\phi \hat{V}_D\) to perform ascent on \(\phi\):

    \[\nabla_\phi \hat{V}_D = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{\nabla_\phi D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)})}{D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)})} - \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{\nabla_\phi D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))}{1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))}\]

    Note that \(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})\) is treated as a fixed input to \(D_\phi\) during the discriminator update — we do not differentiate through \(G_\theta\). The update:

    \[\phi \leftarrow \phi + \eta_D \nabla_\phi \hat{V}_D \tag{67.15}\]

    Generator gradient. We minimize \(\hat{V}_G\) over \(\theta\). The first term \(\frac{1}{m}\sum \ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)})\) is independent of \(\theta\) and contributes zero gradient. Only the second term matters:

    \[\nabla_\theta \hat{V}_G = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \frac{-\nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))}{1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))}\]

    where \(\nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))\) is computed by backpropagation through \(D_\phi\) into \(G_\theta\) (treating \(\phi\) as fixed). The chain rule gives:

    \[\nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) = J_{D_\phi}(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \cdot \nabla_\theta G_\theta(\mathbf{z})\]

    where \(J_{D_\phi}\) is the Jacobian of \(D_\phi\) evaluated at \(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})\). The update:

    \[\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta_G \nabla_\theta \hat{V}_G \tag{67.16}\]

    67.7.4 The Saturating Generator Loss and its Fix

    The saturation problem. The generator loss \(\hat{V}_G = \frac{1}{m}\sum \ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})))\) saturates early in training. When \(G_\theta\) is poorly trained, \(D_\phi\) correctly rejects all generated samples: \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx 0\). Then:

    \[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))) \approx \ln(1 - 0) = \ln 1 = 0\]

    The loss is near \(0\) (its maximum), and its gradient \(\frac{-1}{1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))} \cdot \nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx -\nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))\) can be near zero as well because \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx 0\) means the sigmoid is saturating in the other direction. The generator receives vanishing gradients and barely learns.

    The non-saturating fix (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Instead of minimizing \(\mathbb{E}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))]\), the generator maximizes \(\mathbb{E}[\ln D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))]\):

    \[\hat{L}_G = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ln D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})) \tag{67.17}\]

    When \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx 0\): \(\ln D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx -\infty\), so the loss is large and the gradient \(\frac{-1}{D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))}\nabla_\theta D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))\) is large (note the \(1/D_\phi\) amplification). This provides strong gradients exactly when the generator is performing poorly — precisely when learning is most needed.

    This modification changes the game from a pure minimax to a heuristic objective that no longer exactly minimizes \(D_{\text{JS}}\), but works far better in practice. The theoretical purity is sacrificed for gradient signal.

    Worked Example 67.2: Scalar Case of Gradient Saturation.

    Let \(d = k = 1\). Suppose \(D_\phi(x) = \sigma(w x + b)\) where \(\sigma(t) = 1/(1+e^{-t})\) and \(G_\theta(z) = \theta z\) (scaling only). Early in training, suppose \(\theta \approx 0\), so \(G_\theta(z) \approx 0\) for all \(z\), and \(D_\phi(0) \approx 0.01\) (the discriminator correctly rejects the constant output).

    Saturating loss: \(\nabla_\theta L_G = \frac{-w \cdot D_\phi(0)(1 - D_\phi(0)) \cdot z}{1 - D_\phi(0)} = -w \cdot D_\phi(0) \cdot z \approx -w \cdot 0.01 \cdot z\). The gradient is scaled by \(0.01\) — tiny.

    Non-saturating loss: \(\nabla_\theta L_G = \frac{-w(1 - D_\phi(0)) \cdot z}{D_\phi(0)} \cdot (-1) = \frac{w(1-D_\phi(0))z}{D_\phi(0)} \approx \frac{0.99 \, w \, z}{0.01} = 99 \, w \, z\). The gradient is scaled by \(99\) — nearly 100 times larger.

    67.8 The Complete GAN Training Algorithm

    Algorithm 67.1: GAN Training

    Input: Dataset \(\mathcal{D}\), noise dimension \(k\), batch size \(m\), learning rates \(\eta_D, \eta_G\), number of discriminator steps per generator step \(n_D\).

    Initialize: \(\theta\) (generator parameters), \(\phi\) (discriminator parameters), randomly.

    For each training epoch:

    For each mini-batch \(\{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathcal{D}\):

    Discriminator update (repeat \(n_D\) times): 1. Sample \(\{\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) 2. Compute generated samples: \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(i)} = G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})\) (forward pass, no grad w.r.t. \(\theta\)) 3. Compute discriminator loss: $\(\hat{L}_D = -\frac{1}{m}\!\sum_{i=1}^m \left[\ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}) + \ln(1 - D_\phi(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(i)}))\right]\)$ 4. Update: \(\phi \leftarrow \phi - \eta_D \nabla_\phi \hat{L}_D\) (descent on \(-V\), equivalently ascent on \(V\))

    Generator update (once): 1. Sample fresh \(\{\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) 2. Compute generated samples: \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(i)} = G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})\) (forward pass) 3. Compute generator loss (non-saturating): $\(\hat{L}_G = -\frac{1}{m}\!\sum_{i=1}^m \ln D_\phi(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(i)})\)$ Note: \(\phi\) is held fixed during this step. 4. Update: \(\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta_G \nabla_\theta \hat{L}_G\) (backprop flows through \(D_\phi\) into \(G_\theta\))

    At inference time: Discard \(D_\phi\). For each new sample: draw \(\mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\), return \(G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z})\).

    The ratio \(n_D\): discriminator steps per generator step. A common heuristic is to run more discriminator updates per generator update (e.g., \(n_D = 5\)). The motivation: to provide a good gradient signal to the generator, the discriminator should be "close to optimal" for the current \(G_\theta\). If the generator outpaces the discriminator, the gradients through \(D_\phi\) become uninformative. Conversely, an overpowering discriminator can lead to vanishing generator gradients (the saturation problem). Balancing this ratio is an art as much as a science.

    67.8.1 Why Fresh \(\mathbf{z}\) Samples for the Generator Update?

    During the discriminator update, we compute \(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})\) as fixed inputs to \(D_\phi\) — these \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(i)}\) can be reused. However, for the generator update, we draw fresh \(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\) samples. This is not strictly necessary mathematically, but it reduces correlation between the gradient estimates at successive steps, leading to lower-variance stochastic gradient updates.

    67.9 The Implicit Nature of \(p_\theta\): Why No Density Formula Is Needed

    A conceptually important feature of GANs is that they are implicit generative models: \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) is never written down or evaluated.

    Recall that \(p_\theta\) is defined as the push-forward of \(p_Z = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) through \(G_\theta\). When \(k < d\) (as is typical — a \(100\)-dimensional noise vector generates a \(786432\)-dimensional image), the push-forward \(p_\theta\) is supported on a \(k\)-dimensional manifold embedded in \(\mathbb{R}^d\). This manifold has Lebesgue measure zero in \(\mathbb{R}^d\), so \(p_\theta\) is technically a singular measure — it has no density with respect to the Lebesgue measure. Writing down "\(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\)" as a real-valued function is therefore not possible.

    This is not a problem for the GAN framework because the value function \(V\) only requires: - Sampling from \(p\) (done via \(\mathcal{D}\)), and - Sampling from \(p_\theta\) (done via forward passes through \(G_\theta\)).

    The discriminator's output \(D_\phi(\mathbf{x})\) implicitly encodes the likelihood ratio \(p(\mathbf{x})/p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\), but we never need this ratio in closed form.

    This contrasts sharply with VAEs and autoregressive models, where \(\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) (or a bound on it) must be explicitly computable. The tradeoff: GANs can model lower-dimensional manifolds freely, but cannot be evaluated for held-out likelihood, making quantitative evaluation harder (cf. §66.12).

    67.10 Worked Example: Training a 1D GAN

    To make the abstract algorithm concrete, we trace through a complete 1D example.

    Setup. Let \(p = \mathcal{N}(3, 1)\) (the unknown target) and \(p_Z = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\). Let: $\(G_\theta(z) = \theta_1 z + \theta_2 \quad (\text{affine generator}, \; \theta = (\theta_1, \theta_2))\)$ $\(D_\phi(x) = \sigma(\phi_1 x + \phi_0) \quad (\text{linear discriminator with sigmoid})\)$

    Then \(G_\theta\) pushes \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\) to \(\mathcal{N}(\theta_2, \theta_1^2)\). The target is \(\mathcal{N}(3,1)\), so \(\theta^* = (1, 3)\) (unit scale, shift by 3).

    Initial state. Suppose \(\theta_1 = 0.5\), \(\theta_2 = 0\), so \(p_\theta = \mathcal{N}(0, 0.25)\). The generator is producing samples near 0, far from the target near 3.

    Discriminator at this state. The optimal discriminator (for fixed \(G_\theta\)) is: $\(D^*(x) = \frac{\mathcal{N}(x; 3, 1)}{\mathcal{N}(x; 3, 1) + \mathcal{N}(x; 0, 0.25)}\)$

    For \(x\) near 3: numerator \(\approx 0.399\), denominator \(\approx 0.399 + \approx 0\). So \(D^*(3) \approx 1\) (correctly identified as real). For \(x\) near 0: numerator \(\approx \mathcal{N}(0; 3, 1) \approx 0.0044\), denominator \(\approx 0.0044 + \mathcal{N}(0; 0, 0.25) \approx 0.0044 + 0.798 \approx 0.802\). So \(D^*(0) \approx 0.0055\) (correctly identified as fake).

    Generator gradient. Using the non-saturating loss:

    \[\hat{L}_G = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_i \ln D_\phi(G_\theta(z^{(i)})) = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_i \ln D_\phi(\theta_1 z^{(i)} + \theta_2)\]
    \[\frac{\partial \hat{L}_G}{\partial \theta_2} = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_i \frac{D_\phi'(G_\theta(z^{(i)}))}{D_\phi(G_\theta(z^{(i)}))}\]

    where \(D_\phi'\) is the derivative of \(D_\phi\) with respect to its input. Since \(D^*(0) \approx 0.0055\) and \(D_\phi'(0) = D^*(0)(1 - D^*(0)) \cdot \phi_1 \approx 0.0055 \cdot 0.9945 \cdot \phi_1\):

    \[\frac{\partial \hat{L}_G}{\partial \theta_2} \approx -\phi_1 \cdot (1 - D^*(0))\]

    For the non-saturating loss: \(-1/D^*(0) \cdot D^*(0)(1-D^*(0)) \cdot \phi_1 = -(1-D^*(0))\phi_1 \approx -0.9945\phi_1\).

    This is a strong, non-zero gradient pointing in the direction of increasing \(\theta_2\) (i.e., shifting the generator output toward the real data at \(x = 3\)). Training proceeds with \(\theta_2\) being pushed toward \(3\) and \(\theta_1\) toward \(1\).

    Convergence. At \(\theta^* = (1, 3)\): \(p_\theta = \mathcal{N}(3, 1) = p\). The optimal discriminator becomes \(D^*(x) = 1/2\) everywhere, and \(\hat{L}_G = -\ln(1/2) = \ln 2\) — a constant with zero gradient. Training has converged.

    67.11 Training Difficulties: Mode Collapse and Instability

    The GAN training algorithm described above works in theory but faces serious difficulties in practice. Understanding these difficulties requires understanding the geometry of the optimization problem.

    67.11.1 Mode Collapse

    Definition 67.4 (Mode Collapse). A generator \(G_\theta\) exhibits mode collapse if it produces samples from only a subset of the modes of \(p\), ignoring others.

    Example. If \(p\) is a mixture of five Gaussians (five "modes" — high-probability regions), a mode-collapsed GAN might generate samples from only two of them, leaving the others completely unrepresented.

    Mathematical cause. The Jensen-Shannon divergence, despite being symmetric, can still create incentives for mode collapse due to the discrete nature of the alternating optimization. When the discriminator is near-optimal and the generator is far from \(p\), the discriminator provides a very small gradient signal for modes the generator has not yet learned — the generator has no incentive to explore them. The generator gravitates to modes where it already generates plausible-looking samples and finds those regions locally stable.

    A deeper analysis shows that when \(p\) and \(p_\theta\) have disjoint support (common early in training), \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta) = \ln 2\) regardless of the specific geometry of the mismatch — the gradient of \(D_{\text{JS}}\) with respect to the generator parameters is zero almost everywhere. This is the vanishing gradient problem for the theoretically correct objective. The practical non-saturating loss alleviates the symptom but does not address the underlying cause.

    67.11.2 Training Instability

    Because the alternating optimization does not have convergence guarantees, GANs often exhibit oscillatory behavior: the generator and discriminator chase each other around a cycle rather than converging to the saddle point.

    The root cause is that the saddle-point problem (67.10) is in general a non-convex-non-concave optimization: \(V\) is non-convex in \(\theta\) (due to \(G_\theta\) being a deep network) and non-concave in \(\phi\) (due to \(D_\phi\) being a deep network). In this regime, gradient descent-ascent can cycle indefinitely.

    67.11.3 The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN): A Cure

    The Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) addresses both problems by replacing \(D_{\text{JS}}\) with the Wasserstein-1 distance (also called Earth Mover's distance):

    \[W_1(p, q) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(p,q)} \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \sim \gamma}[\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|]\]

    where \(\Pi(p,q)\) is the set of all joint distributions with marginals \(p\) and \(q\). \(W_1\) is well-defined even when supports are disjoint (unlike \(D_{\text{JS}}\), which saturates to \(\ln 2\)) and provides gradients everywhere.

    Via the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality theorem:

    \[W_1(p, q) = \sup_{\|f\|_L \leq 1} \left\{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[f(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim q}[f(\mathbf{x})]\right\}\]

    where the supremum is over all \(1\)-Lipschitz functions \(f\). Parameterizing \(f\) by a neural network \(D_\phi\) constrained to be \(1\)-Lipschitz (enforced via weight clipping or gradient penalty) gives the WGAN objective:

    \[\min_\theta \max_{\phi : \|D_\phi\|_L \leq 1} \left\{\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[D_\phi(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))]\right\}\]

    Note that \(D_\phi\) is no longer a classifier outputting probabilities — it is an unconstrained real-valued function (the WGAN paper calls it a "critic," not a "discriminator," to emphasize this). The mathematical development of WGAN follows the same structure as this chapter but uses the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality in place of the Jensen-Shannon variational representation.

    67.12 Inference: The Generator Stands Alone

    Once training is complete, the discriminator is discarded entirely. It was a computational device for constructing and optimizing the bound on the divergence metric — a teacher, not a permanent part of the model. The generative model is simply:

    \[\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z}), \qquad \mathbf{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k) \tag{67.18}\]

    This is a single forward pass through a neural network — computationally fast and independent of the dataset \(\mathcal{D}\). The discriminator was useful only insofar as it guided \(\theta\) to the correct value \(\theta^*\); once that is achieved, it has nothing more to contribute.

    This stands in contrast to non-parametric methods (like kernel density estimation) where the training data must be retained and consulted at inference time. A trained GAN compresses the knowledge of \(p\) entirely into \(\theta^*\), a fixed parameter vector.

    67.13 Summary

    This chapter developed the GAN framework in full mathematical detail. The essential arc is:

    The objective is to minimize a divergence \(D(p \| p_\theta)\). We choose the \(f\)-divergence family, and specifically the Jensen-Shannon divergence. This divergence is intractable (neither \(p\) nor \(p_\theta\) is available in closed form), so we use the Fenchel conjugate (variational) representation of \(f\)-divergences to construct a lower bound:

    \[D_f(p \| p_\theta) \geq \sup_\phi \left\{\mathbb{E}_p[T_\phi(\mathbf{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[f^*(T_\phi(\mathbf{x}))]\right\}\]

    Tightening this bound requires solving an inner maximization over \(\phi\), the parameters of a second neural network \(D_\phi\). Substituting the JS divergence, the optimal inner solution is the Bayes-optimal classifier \(D^*(\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x})/(p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x}))\), and the bound exactly equals \(2D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta) - 2\ln 2\).

    The resulting optimization is a saddle-point (minimax) problem over \((\theta, \phi)\), solved by alternating gradient descent-ascent. The non-saturating generator loss addresses the practical problem of vanishing gradients early in training. Training is inherently difficult because the saddle point must be sought in a non-convex-non-concave landscape, leading to instability and mode collapse.

    At inference, the discriminator is discarded. The generator \(G_{\theta^*}\) maps Gaussian noise to approximate samples from \(p\) in a single forward pass.

    The GAN framework instantiates the general principle that to learn to sample from an unknown distribution, one need only learn a deterministic transformation from a simple known distribution. Everything else — the discriminator, the minimax game, the alternating updates — is machinery for identifying the correct transformation from data alone.

    End of Chapter 67. Chapter 68 develops Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), which address the same generative modeling goal using an explicit lower bound on the log-likelihood (the ELBO), introducing a learned encoder alongside the decoder to make the bound tractable.
