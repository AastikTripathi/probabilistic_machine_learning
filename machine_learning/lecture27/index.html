    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 18: The Empirical Risk Minimization Paradigm and the Foundations of Linear Regression

    18.1 The Grand Duality: ERM vs. Density Estimation

    Machine learning architecture can be approached from two distinct perspectives: the Probabilistic Paradigm and the Risk Minimization Framework. While separate engineering sub-communities often work exclusively within one viewpoint, they are fundamentally solving the exact same underlying optimization problems under different mathematical names.

      [ The Distribution Estimation Approach ]                 [ The Empirical Risk Minimization Approach ]
  - Objective: Estimate a density landscape.               - Objective: Approximate an functional mapping engine.
  - Optimization: Minimize KL Divergence / Maximize MLE.   - Optimization: Minimize average loss over a sample ledger.
  - Target Parameter: Density weights (θ).                 - Target Parameter: Hypothesis coefficients (θ).

    1. The Probabilistic Formulation

    Given an empirical data folder \(D\), we treat our observations as realizations of random variables. The core task is to model the background probability density functions by finding a parameter vector \(\theta^*\) that minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the true natural distribution \(p_{\text{true}}\) and our parametric model distribution \(p_\theta\):

    \[\theta^* = \arg\min_{\theta \in \Theta} D_{\text{KL}}\left(p_{\text{true}} \parallel p_\theta\right) \equiv \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \ln p_\theta(\vec{v}_i)\]

    2. The Functional Risk Formulation

    Given the exact same data folder \(D\), we step out of density language and cast the problem as learning a deterministic function \(h: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{Y}\). This mapping engine belongs to a targeted Hypothesis Class (\(\mathcal{H}\)). We define a non-negative Loss Function (\(L\)) to grade individual estimation errors, and we find our optimal function \(\hat{h}^*\) by minimizing the arithmetic sample average of those penalties—a framework known as Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM):

    \[\hat{h}^* = \arg\min_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \hat{R}_N(h) \equiv \arg\min_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} L\left(h(\vec{x}_i), \,\, y_i\right)\]

    18.2 The Continuous Gaussian Duality Case Study

    To see this underlying connection clearly, let us analyze a supervised regression scenario where our features span a multi-axis continuum \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\) and our label is a continuous real scalar \(y \in \mathbb{R}^1\).

    Suppose we tackle this from a probabilistic perspective and state that the true conditional posterior distribution \(p(y \mid \vec{x})\) follows a homoscedastic continuous Gaussian Distribution whose moving mean center (\(\mu\)) is driven by a hypothesis function \(h_\theta(\vec{x})\), while its variance is locked to identity (\(\sigma^2 = \mathbf{I}\)):

    \[p_\theta(y \mid \vec{x}) = \mathcal{N}\left(y; \,\, \mu = h_\theta(\vec{x}), \,\, \sigma^2 = 1\right)\]

    If we expand the continuous probability density function for this normal distribution and isolate our target variable, our maximum likelihood optimization problem becomes:

    \[\theta^* = \arg\max_{\theta} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \ln \left[ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\left(y_i - h_\theta(\vec{x}_i)\right)^2\right) \right]\]

    Applying basic logarithmic expansion rules simplifies this equation cleanly:

    \[\theta^* = \arg\max_{\theta} \left[ N \ln\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\right) - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(y_i - h_\theta(\vec{x}_i)\right)^2 \right]\]

    Dropping the static normalization constant and inverting the sign to convert the maximization goal into a minimization routine isolates the final optimization requirement:

    \[\theta^* = \arg\min_{\theta} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(y_i - h_\theta(\vec{x}_i)\right)^2\]

    This derivation yields a definitive relationship: Running Empirical Risk Minimization with a Squared Error Loss function is the mathematically exact equivalent of maximizing the likelihood of a conditional Gaussian distribution.

    Deconstructing the "Noise" Conception: In deterministic textbooks, this relationship is frequently introduced using a generative noise formula: $\(y = h_\theta(\vec{x}) + \epsilon, \quad \text{where } \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\)$

    The professor points out that using the word "noise" adds unnecessary confusion. This formula is simply a roundabout way of stating that the spatial conditional density of the target variable is a standard Gaussian distribution centered directly on the output of your hypothesis function. Shifting a zero-mean normal curve by a deterministic value \(h_\theta(\vec{x})\) shifts the distribution's mean center while preserving its geometric shape.

    18.3 The Geometry of the Linear Family Model

    Now that our optimization frameworks are aligned, we introduce the most fundamental family of functional mapping engines: Linear Models.

    Let our input features exist as a multi-axis continuous column vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\). A linear hypothesis model approximates the continuous target value \(y\) by computing a weighted combination of each individual feature dimension, shifted by a baseline intercept constant (\(\theta_0\), known as the Bias Term):

    \[h(\vec{x}) = \theta_1 x_1 + \theta_2 x_2 + \dots + \theta_D x_D + \theta_0\]

    From a learning perspective, each parameter coefficient acts as a scaling dial. It isolates and calculates the specific predictive contribution that an individual feature measurement adds to the final system decision.

    The Input Augmentation Trick

    To simplify our matrix equations, we get rid of the separate bias parameter \(\theta_0\) by applying a standard geometric transformation. We append a structural constant value of \(1\) directly to the front of our feature coordinates to build an augmented vector space:

    \[\vec{x}_{\text{augmented}} = [1, \,\, x_1, \,\, x_2, \dots, x_D]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D+1}\]
    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{augmented}} = [\theta_0, \,\, \theta_1, \,\, \theta_2, \dots, \theta_D]^T \in \mathbb{R}^{D+1}\]

    By augmenting our vector space, the entire affine linear equation compresses into a single vector inner product (dot product):

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\]

    For the remainder of this chapter, we use this augmented convention implicitly, tracking our variables inside a clean \((D+1)\)-dimensional real vector space.

    18.4 Mathematical Derivation of Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)

    We now combine our augmented linear hypothesis class (\(h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\)) with our empirical squared error risk function to formulate our global optimization problem:

    \[\vec{\theta}^* = \arg\min_{\vec{\theta}} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}_i - y_i \right)^2\]

    To solve this efficiently using linear algebra, we stack our independent data observations and targets into unified matrices.

    Step 1: Construct the Global Design Matrix

    Let \(\mathbf{X}\) represent our global Design Matrix. We stack our \(N\) augmented row vectors horizontally to build an \(N \times (D+1)\) rectangular coordinate array. Let \(\vec{Y}\) represent our target column vector of length \(N\):

    \[\mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} (\vec{x}_1)^T \\ (\vec{x}_2)^T \\ \vdots \\ (\vec{x}_N)^T \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & x_{1,1} & \dots & x_{1,D} \\ 1 & x_{2,1} & \dots & x_{2,D} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{N,1} & \dots & x_{N,D} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times (D+1)}, \quad \vec{Y} = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_N \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^N\]

    Step 2: Formulate the Matrix Loss Functional

    Using these stacked linear algebra structures, our summation of squared errors converts into a single squared Euclidean vector norm:

    \[J(\vec{\theta}) = \frac{1}{N} \|\mathbf{X}\vec{\theta} - \vec{Y}\|_2^2\]

    We expand this squared norm into its explicit transpose vector multiplication format, ignoring the constant scalar multiplier \(\frac{1}{N}\) since it does not shift the coordinates of the global minimum:

    \[J(\vec{\theta}) = (\mathbf{X}\vec{\theta} - \vec{Y})^T (\mathbf{X}\vec{\theta} - \vec{Y})\]

    Distributing the transpose matrix operator across the internal components yields:

    \[J(\vec{\theta}) = \left( (\mathbf{X}\vec{\theta})^T - \vec{Y}^T \right) (\mathbf{X}\vec{\theta} - \vec{Y})\]
    \[J(\vec{\theta}) = \vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta} - \vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y} - \vec{Y}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta} + \vec{Y}^T \vec{Y}\]

    Because the internal vector products evaluate to scalar real constants, the middle terms are equivalents (\(\vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y} \equiv \vec{Y}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta}\)), allowing us to merge them cleanly:

    \[J(\vec{\theta}) = \vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta} - 2\vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y} + \vec{Y}^T \vec{Y}\]

    Step 3: Compute the Vector Gradient

    Because our global cost function \(J(\vec{\theta})\) is a smooth, convex, scalar-valued function curving over a vector parameter domain, we can compute its exact multi-axis derivative vector (the Gradient). We apply standard matrix calculus identities to calculate the gradient with respect to our parameter column \(\vec{\theta}\):

    \[\nabla_{\vec{\theta}} J(\vec{\theta}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \vec{\theta}} \left[ \vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta} \right] - \frac{\partial}{\partial \vec{\theta}} \left[ 2\vec{\theta}^T \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y} \right] + \frac{\partial}{\partial \vec{\theta}} \left[ \vec{Y}^T \vec{Y} \right]\]
    \[\nabla_{\vec{\theta}} J(\vec{\theta}) = 2\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta} - 2\mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y}\]

    Step 4: Isolate the Closed-Form Normal Equations

    To locate the absolute minimum valley of this quadratic bowl, we set our multi-axis gradient vector equal to the zero vector (\(\vec{0}\)):

    \[2\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta}^* - 2\mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y} = \vec{0}\]

    Dividing by \(2\) and moving the data targets to the right-hand side isolates the system of Normal Equations:

    \[\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \vec{\theta}^* = \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y}\]

    Assuming the square matrix product \(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X}\) is non-singular and has full column rank, we invert the operator to the left side, extracting our definitive closed-form solution:

    \[\vec{\theta}^* = \left( \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T \vec{Y}\]

    This completes the derivation. The matrix combination \(\left( \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{X} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T\) is known as the Moore-Penrose Pseudo-Inverse of our design matrix. Geometrically, this equation projects our target vector \(\vec{Y}\) orthogonally directly onto the linear column space spanned by our feature coordinates, finding the unique optimal parameter configuration that minimizes our squared error risk.

    18.5 Summary Reference Sheet

    The Core Unifying Principles

    1. The Core Equivalence: Optimizing an empirical risk function over an observed dataset matrix is identical to running maximum likelihood estimation across an aligned probability distribution family.
    2. The Structural Bias Rule: When you run a linear regression model using squared error loss, you are making a foundational assumption that your real-world data noise follows a continuous Gaussian distribution centered on a linear hyperplane.
    3. The Algebraic Loophole: Because the OLS matrix objective is perfectly smooth and convex, we do not need slow, iterative optimization loops. We calculate the exact vector gradient, set it to zero, and evaluate the optimal parameters instantly via the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse matrix equation.
