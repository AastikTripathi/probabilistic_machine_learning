    Chapter 23: The \(k\)-Nearest Neighbors Support Manifold and the Density-Driven Proof of Heuristic Classification

    23.1 Comprehensive Notation Glossary

    To maintain complete concept clearance and track every structural index across this non-parametric derivation, use this table as your master key.

    Symbol Mathematical Object Structural Identity / Definition
    \(\vec{x}\) Real Vector The target query coordinate point in space where density or class is evaluated; \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\).
    \(N\) Integer Constant The total global count of sample rows available inside the training dataset ledger.
    \(V\) Scalar Variable The dynamic hypervolume space centered at \(\vec{x}\) that grows or contracts across the support manifold.
    \(k\) Integer Hyperparameter The fixed, pre-chosen number of neighbor points required to halt hypervolume growth.
    \(M\) Integer Constant The total number of unique discrete classification categories available in the target space.
    \(k_i\) Integer Variable The localized count of sample points within the active hypervolume \(V\) that belong specifically to class \(i\).
    \(p(\vec{x}, Y = i)\) Probability Density The non-parametric joint probability density of encountering features \(\vec{x}\) and class label \(i\).
    \(P(Y = i \mid \vec{x})\) Probability Mass The continuous conditional posterior probability mapping that the true label is class \(i\) given coordinate \(\vec{x}\).

    23.2 The Geometry of Inverse Space Scaling: Fixing \(k\) and Growing \(V\)

    In Chapter 17, we formulated the baseline non-parametric density estimation ratio:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{k}{N \cdot V}\]

    We evaluated Path A: The Parzen Window, where we locked the local search hypervolume (\(V = h^D\)) as a static constant and counted the fluctuating number of data points (\(k\)) that happened to fall inside its boundaries.

    The professor points out a critical engineering bug with Parzen windows in high-dimensional spaces: sparsity and empty bins. Because data vectors sit sparsely across high-dimensional manifolds, fixing a small volume \(V\) often results in many empty regions where \(k = 0\), causing your density estimates to collapse to zero. While smoothing filters help soften these step-function drops, they do not resolve the underlying tracking limitation.

    To fix this, we pivot to Path B: The \(k\)-Nearest Neighbors (\(k\)-NN) Density Estimator. Instead of fixing the volume, we invert the operational roles of our variables:

    1. We lock the target sample count (\(k\)) as a fixed integer hyperparameter.
    2. We allow the local hypervolume \(V\) around our evaluation point \(\vec{x}\) to act as a dynamic variable.

    To evaluate the density \(\hat{p}(\vec{x})\) at a query point, you draw a microscopic hypersphere centered at \(\vec{x}\) and allow its boundary to grow outward across the metric space. The moment the expanding volume captures exactly \(k\) data points from your training dataset folder, the growth halts.

    • In high-density regions of space, the volume lands on a tight, compact value because \(k\) neighbors are found close by. The ratio yields a high density score.
    • In sparse, low-density regions, the volume expands wide before capturing its quota, resulting in a large value for \(V\) that pushes the density calculation down.

    23.3 The Core Derivation: Connecting \(k\)-NN Density to Bayes Classification

    The professor delivers a major structural insight by demonstrating that the popular \(k\)-NN classification heuristic is actually a Bayes Optimal Classifier running over non-parametric density estimates. Let let us walk through this proof step-by-step with zero skipped algebra.

    Step 1: Set up the Multi-Class Space Partitioning

    We are given an \(M\)-class classification environment (\(Y \in \{1, 2, \dots, M\}\)) containing \(N\) total data rows distributed across our feature domain \(\mathbb{R}^D\).

    We pass a raw query coordinate \(\vec{x}\) into our system. We expand a dynamic hypervolume \(V\) around \(\vec{x}\) until it captures exactly \(k\) sample rows. Inside this captured pool of \(k\) neighbors, we tally the number of points belonging to each individual category. Let \(k_i\) represent the count of points belonging to class \(i\). Because the total number of captured points is fixed to \(k\), these internal counts sum up to our hyperparameter target:

    \[\sum_{i=1}^{M} k_i = k\]

    Step 2: Formulate the Joint Non-Parametric Density Estimate

    Using our inverse space scaling ratio, the non-parametric estimate for the Joint Probability Density of encountering features \(\vec{x}\) matching class label \(i\) is calculated directly by looking at the class-specific sample counts captured within our local volume:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}, Y = i) = \frac{k_i}{N \cdot V}\]

    The Step-by-Step Validation: If you deconstruct this joint density using classical pattern recognition properties (such as the identities found in Pattern Classification by Duda, Hart, and Stork), the derivation unfolds through class-conditional layers. Let \(N_i\) represent the total number of samples in the entire dataset that belong to class \(i\). The class-conditional density is written as: $\(\hat{p}(\vec{x} \mid Y = i) = \frac{k_i}{N_i \cdot V}\)$

    The discrete prior baseline probability for class \(i\) is calculated using empirical relative frequencies: $\(\hat{P}(Y = i) = \frac{N_i}{N}\)$

    Multiplying the class-conditional density by the prior yields the exact joint density equation stated by the professor: $\(\hat{p}(\vec{x}, Y = i) = \hat{p}(\vec{x} \mid Y = i) \cdot \hat{P}(Y = i) = \frac{k_i}{\cancel{N_i} \cdot V} \cdot \frac{\cancel{N_i}}{N} = \frac{k_i}{N \cdot V}\)$

    Step 3: Compute the Latent Posterior Mass

    To build a Bayes Optimal Classifier, our model must evaluate the conditional posterior probability \(P(Y = i \mid \vec{x})\) for each class and select whichever category holds the dominant mass. We invoke Bayes' rule to expand this conditional fraction:

    \[\hat{P}(Y = i \mid \vec{x}) = \frac{\hat{p}(\vec{x}, Y = i)}{\hat{p}(\vec{x})}\]

    The denominator \(\hat{p}(\vec{x})\) is the total marginal density across our feature space, which is evaluated by summing our joint density components across all \(M\) classes:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{M} \hat{p}(\vec{x}, Y = j) = \sum_{j=1}^{M} \frac{k_j}{N \cdot V}\]

    We pull the constant factor \(\frac{1}{N \cdot V}\) out of the summation:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{N \cdot V} \sum_{j=1}^{M} k_j\]

    Because the class-specific counts sum to our fixed hyperparameter quota (\(\sum_{j=1}^M k_j = k\)), the total marginal density simplifies cleanly to:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{k}{N \cdot V}\]

    We substitute our joint density expression (Step 2) and our marginal density expression back into our Bayes posterior fraction:

    \[\hat{P}(Y = i \mid \vec{x}) = \frac{\frac{k_i}{N \cdot V}}{\frac{k}{N \cdot V}}\]

    We cancel out the shared global data size \(N\) and the dynamic hypervolume variable \(V\) from the numerator and denominator:

    \[\hat{P}(Y = i \mid \vec{x}) = \frac{k_i}{\cancel{N \cdot V}} \cdot \frac{\cancel{N \cdot V}}{k} \implies \hat{P}(Y = i \mid \vec{x}) = \frac{k_i}{k}\]

    Look at that final equation. The posterior probability that our query point belongs to class \(i\) is simply the fraction of its \(k\) nearest neighbors that carry that label.

    Step 4: The Decision Rule Convergence

    We pass this clean posterior formulation directly into our universal Bayes Decision Rule (derived in Chapter 12). The system assigns the query point to the class that maximizes the local posterior mass:

    \[h^*(\vec{x}) = \arg\max_{i \in \{1, \dots, M\}} \hat{P}(Y = i \mid \vec{x}) \implies h^*(\vec{x}) = \arg\max_{i \in \{1, \dots, M\}} \frac{k_i}{k}\]

    Because the hyperparameter denominator \(k\) is a positive constant shared across all classes, it has zero effect on the location of the maximum peak. We drop it from the optimization operator:

    \[h^*(\vec{x}) = \arg\max_{i \in \{1, \dots, M\}} k_i\]

    23.4 The Absolute Punchline of Non-Parametric Modeling

    This convergence delivers the final realization of the lecture:

    The Grand Unifying Law of \(k\)-NN: The standard \(k\)-Nearest Neighbors Classifier is not an arbitrary, ungrounded heuristic invented by programmers to vote on nearby dots. It is the exact mathematical manifestation of a Bayes Optimal Classifier running over non-parametric density fields estimated via inverse space scaling.

    This perspective re-contextualizes the role of the hyperparameter \(k\). When you select the number of neighbors in code, you are not just choosing a voting pool; you are directly controlling the structural resolution of your non-parametric density estimation loop.

    23.5 Theoretical Performance Bounds: The Asymptotic Safety Floor

    The professor highlights a powerful classical theorem derived in pattern recognition literature (formalized by Cover and Hart) to prove the safety of this non-parametric approach.

    Let \(R^*\) represent the absolute theoretical minimum risk achieved by a perfect Bayes classifier running with complete knowledge of the universe's true distributions (\(h_B\)). Let \(R_{1\text{-NN}}\) represent the true risk achieved by a simple 1-Nearest Neighbor Classifier (\(k=1\)) running under an infinite sample size limit (\(N \to \infty\)).

    Under a binary classification manifold, the asymptotic risk is tightly bounded by the following inequality:

    \[R^* \leq R_{1\text{-NN}} \leq 2R^*\left(1 - R^*\right) \leq 2R^*\]

    This theorem provides an exceptional baseline guarantee for machine learning engineering: the error rate of a simple 1-Nearest Neighbor classifier is asymptotically bounded at most to twice the absolute ideal Bayes error rate. If you deploy an extremely complex deep learning architecture that scores worse than a simple nearest neighbor lookup on your data repository, you can safely discard it. The nearest neighbor baseline represents the simplest, non-parametric asset available to validate your classification space.
