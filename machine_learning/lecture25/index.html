    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 17: Non-Parametric Density Estimation and Parzen Window Foundations

    17.1 The Departure from Parametric Paradigms

    Up to this point, our entire exploration of density estimation has been explicitly parametric. Whether we assumed the environment followed a single Multivariate Gaussian profile (\(\mathcal{N}(\vec{\mu}, \Sigma)\)), a discrete Multinoulli simplex, or a compound Gaussian Mixture Model (\(\sum \alpha_j \mathcal{N}_j\)), we forced our optimization engine to operate under a rigid constraint: we assumed a fixed global structural family a priori. Under that assumption, training meant running data through a divergence-minimization loop to lock down a permanent parameter vector \(\theta\). Once \(\theta\) was saved, the training data points were discarded; the model evaluated new points in constant time \(O(1)\) based entirely on that fixed functional shape.

    However, parametric modeling hits a hard wall when the underlying data topology is highly irregular, asymmetrical, or completely unknown. If you make a faulty structural assumption about your distribution family, your model will suffer from permanent bias.

      [ Parametric Estimation ]                       [ Non-Parametric Estimation ]
  - Assume a global functional family.           - Make zero structural family assumptions.
  - Optimize a fixed parameter set θ.            - Retain the entire training dataset matrix.
  - Constant inference time cost O(1).           - Evaluate density locally at target coordinate x.

    To resolve this limitation, we introduce Non-Parametric Density Estimation. In this framework, we make zero assumptions about the global parametric family of the data. Furthermore, we do not attempt to learn a permanent, static formula for the entire space.

    Instead, we shift our engineering objective: we evaluate the probability density locally at a specific target coordinate point \(\vec{x}\) by analyzing the physical distribution of our sample instances directly around that coordinate.

    For a downstream task like building a Bayes Optimal Classifier (\(h_B(\vec{x}) = \arg\max P(Y=c \mid \vec{x})\)), this local evaluation is all we actually need. We do not need a complete analytical equation for the entire universe; we only need an accurate calculation of the class-conditional density value \(p(\vec{x} \mid Y=c)\) at the specific real-time query vector currently passing through the system.

    17.2 The Geometric Derivation of Local Density

    To build a non-parametric estimator from first principles, we analyze how continuous probability masses distribute over localized spaces.

    Let \(\vec{X} \in \mathbb{R}^D\) be a continuous random vector governed by an unknown probability density function \(p(\vec{x})\). Let \(\mathcal{R}\) represent a bounded hypervolume region sliced out of the support space of \(p(\vec{x})\). The total probability mass \(P\) accumulating inside this localized region is calculated as the continuous volume integral:

    \[P = \int_{\mathcal{R}} p(\vec{x}') \, d\vec{x}'\]

    Suppose we shrink this spatial region \(\mathcal{R}\) until it forms a very tight, microscopic volume around our target evaluation coordinate \(\vec{x}\). If the volume is sufficiently small, we can make a safe continuity assumption: the true density function \(p(\vec{x})\) is approximately constant across the interior of the region. Under this assumption, we can pull the constant density value out of the integral:

    \[P \approx p(\vec{x}) \int_{\mathcal{R}} 1 \, d\vec{x}' \implies P \approx p(\vec{x}) \cdot V\]

    Where \(V \triangleq \int_{\mathcal{R}} 1 \, d\vec{x}'\) is the exact geometric hypervolume (length in \(\mathbb{R}^1\), area in \(\mathbb{R}^2\), volume in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)) of our chosen region \(\mathcal{R}\).

    Now, let us analyze this from an empirical perspective. Suppose we harvest a dataset \(D\) containing \(N\) independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) vector rows drawn from this environment:

    \[D = \{\vec{x}_1, \vec{x}_2, \dots, \vec{x}_N\} \sim \text{i.i.d. } p(\vec{x})\]

    Every individual data point \(\vec{x}_i\) passing through space has a binary choice: it either lands inside our specified local region \(\mathcal{R}\) with true success probability \(P\), or it lands outside with failure probability \(1 - P\). Because our data rows are gathered i.i.d., the total count of samples \(k\) that happen to fall inside our region is governed by a standard Binomial Distribution:

    \[k \sim \text{Binomial}(N, \,\, P)\]

    Invoking our discrete maximum likelihood estimator derived in Chapter 15, the empirical success probability is simply the relative count of observed successes over the total sample size:

    \[\hat{P}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{k}{N}\]

    To extract our final density value, we equate our continuous physical approximation with our discrete empirical MLE profile:

    \[p(\vec{x}) \cdot V \approx \frac{k}{N} \implies \hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{k}{N \cdot V}\]

    This is the foundational equation of non-parametric density estimation. It states that the density at any coordinate point is simply the ratio of localized sample occurrences (\(k\)) to the total global data mass (\(N\)) scaled by the local search volume (\(V\)).

    17.3 The Dual Structural Paths: Parzen Windows vs. \(k\)-NN

    To transform this fundamental ratio (\(\hat{p} = \frac{k}{NV}\)) into an executable tracking program, an engineer must manage two competing variables simultaneously: the hypervolume size \(V\) and the internal sample count \(k\). This introduces a major structural choice, leading to two distinct non-parametric strategies:

                              [ Non-Parametric Density Ratio: k / (N·V) ]
                                               |
                 ---------------------------------------------------------
                 |                                                       |
   [ Path A: The Parzen Window ]                           [ Path B: k-Nearest Neighbors ]
   - Lock down a fixed hypervolume V.                      - Lock down a fixed target count k.
   - Count how many points k fall inside.                  - Grow the hypervolume V until it captures k points.

    In the rest of this chapter, we focus on Path A: The Parzen Window approach (also known as Kernel Density Estimation).

    17.4 The Parzen Window Estimator (The Hypercube Kernel)

    To calculate our counts cleanly across a multivariate space \(\mathbb{R}^D\), we define our region \(\mathcal{R}\) as a symmetric \(D\)-dimensional Hypercube centered perfectly on our target evaluation coordinate \(\vec{x}\). We declare that this hypercube has a fixed edge side-length of \(h\). Under this structural constraint, the total geometric hypervolume of our search zone is written as:

    \[V = h^D\]

    To programmatically count how many data rows \(\vec{x}_i\) from our training folder fall within the boundaries of this hypercube, we construct a unit indicator function known as the Window Function (or Kernel), denoted as \(\phi(\vec{u})\):

    \[\phi(\vec{u}) \triangleq \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } |u_j| \leq \frac{1}{2} \quad \forall \,\, j = 1, 2, \dots, D \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

    This function draws a perfect unit hypercube centered at the coordinate origin, returning a \(1.0\) if an incoming vector lands inside its walls and a \(0.0\) if it falls outside.

    To shift this search zone so that it centers on our active query point \(\vec{x}\) and scales to match our chosen edge length \(h\), we alter the internal vector arguments. For any specific training data row \(\vec{x}_i\), we evaluate:

    \[\text{Count Component} = \phi\left( \frac{\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i}{h} \right)\]

    This is where the transcript's notation broke down. Let us clarify the vector calculus scaling: you divide the coordinate shift vector element-wise by the linear side length \(h\), not the global hypervolume \(h^D\). This maps the distance between the two points to our unit window scale.

    By summing this indicator function across our entire training ledger of \(N\) rows, we calculate the exact total count of samples \(k\) residing inside our spatial hypercube boundary:

    \[k = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \phi\left( \frac{\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i}{h} \right)\]

    Substituting this counting summation and our hypervolume expression (\(V = h^D\)) back into our core non-parametric density ratio yields the definitive formula for the Parzen Window Density Estimator:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{N \cdot h^D} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \phi\left( \frac{\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i}{h} \right)\]

    17.5 From Hard Bins to Smooth Kernels (Kernel Density Estimation)

    If you implement the hypercube window function \(\phi(\vec{u})\) exactly as derived above, you are running an automated multidimensional Histogram. While this works, it introduces a major engineering drawback: the resulting density surface is highly discontinuous.

    Because the hypercube kernel utilizes a hard binary switch (returning exactly \(0\) or \(1\)), your density calculations will experience sharp step-function jumps. If a query point \(\vec{x}\) moves slightly across space, causing a training sample to pop outside the hypercube wall, the estimated density score drops instantly, creating jagged, un-differentiable boundaries.

    To generate a smooth, continuous density surface, we replace the rigid binary hypercube function with a smooth, continuous, and twice-differentiable window function. This approach is known as Kernel Density Estimation (KDE).

    Our primary choice for a smooth window function is the standard Multivariate Gaussian Kernel:

    \[\phi(\vec{u}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{D/2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} \|\vec{u}\|_2^2 \right)\]

    Substituting this smooth normal kernel back into our global Parzen equation yields a continuous density surface:

    \[\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{N \cdot h^D} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{D/2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} \left\| \frac{\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i}{h} \right\|_2^2 \right)\]

    Clarifying the Duality: KDE vs. GMMs

    The student at the end of the lecture boards raises an excellent question: If this equation places a Gaussian bell curve over our data points and adds them together, isn't this just a Gaussian Mixture Model?

    While they look mathematically similar, they are driven by completely different operational principles:

    Architectural Dimension Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)
    Component Centers Learned dynamically by running the iterative EM algorithm (\(\vec{\mu}_j\)). Locked directly onto the actual coordinates of your training data rows (\(\vec{x}_i\)).
    Component Scale (\(M\)) A compact hyperparameter choice tracking a small number of sub-populations (\(M \ll N\)). Scales exactly with the total number of samples in your dataset (\(M \equiv N\)).
    Variance Control Individual covariance matrices optimized from data (\(\Sigma_j\)). A single, fixed smoothing hyperparameter \(h\) (known as the Bandwidth) set by the engineer.

    17.6 The Computational Complexity Penalty

    Non-parametric modeling grants you total freedom from faulty structural assumptions, but it exacts a severe toll on your application's computing infrastructure.

    In a parametric model (like a GMM), once training is complete, the data points are deleted. Evaluating a new point requires running a small, constant-time calculation \(O(M)\) against your learned parameters.

    In a non-parametric model, there is no training phase. The dataset is the model. To evaluate the density at a single query coordinate \(\vec{x}\), your backend must calculate the distance between that query vector and every single sample row stored in your training matrix.

    \[\text{Inference Time Complexity} = O(N \cdot D)\]

    If your enterprise application manages a dataset of \(N = 10,000,000\) consumer rows spanning \(D = 500\) distinct operational features, running this non-parametric summation loop for every real-time query passing through your API will heavily strain your infrastructure. Non-parametric models completely trade away inference speed to gain absolute topological flexibility.

    Textbook Notes Study Guide

    The Generalist's Non-Parametric Laws: 1. The Core Evaluation Shift: Non-parametric methods bypass the need to define a global structural distribution family. They evaluate density locally at a specific query coordinate \(\vec{x}\) based on sample density within a local hypervolume. 2. The Parzen Window Mechanism: Implements local estimation by locking a fixed hypervolume \(V = h^D\) and calculating the count of intersecting sample vectors via a kernel window function: \(\hat{p}(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{N h^D}\sum \phi(\cdot)\). 3. The Instance-Based Penalty: Non-parametric models require zero training cycles but impose a linear computational bottleneck (\(O(N \cdot D)\)) at inference time because the model must look up and compare every saved historical row for every new query.

    The foundation of non-parametric instance-based evaluation is now fully mapped. We have derived the local spatial ratio, formalized the Parzen hypercube window vector scaling, and analyzed the computational trade-offs separating kernels from global mixture optimizations.

    You spotted exactly what is happening under the hood. Non-parametric density estimation (specifically Kernel Density Estimation and Parzen windows) is not just a collection of clever statistical tricks; it is Applied Functional Analysis masquerading as data science. When we move away from fixed parameters, we stop optimizing over coordinate vectors and start operating inside infinite-dimensional function spaces.

    1. The Metric Space Foundation (\((M, d)\))

    Recall the definition of a Metric Space: a set \(M\) paired with a distance function (metric) \(d: M \times M \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) that satisfies non-negativity, identity of indiscernibles, symmetry, and the triangle inequality.

    Look at the core argument inside our smooth Parzen Window (KDE) formula from the last chapter:

    \[\left\| \frac{\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i}{h} \right\|_2\]

    That norm \(\|\cdot\|_2\) is an unyielding metric function \(d(\vec{x}, \vec{x}_i)\) mapping our feature space. Non-parametric estimation is completely dependent on the geometry of metric spaces.

    • We are making a foundational topological assumption: if the metric distance between a query point \(\vec{x}\) and a training sample \(\vec{x}_i\) approaches zero, their local probability masses must be highly correlated.
    • If you change the metric (e.g., switching from the \(L_2\) Euclidean norm to an \(L_1\) Manhattan metric or a Mahalanobis distance), you completely alter the underlying geometric topology of the density landscape.

    2. Moving into Function Spaces: Hilbert Spaces and RKHS

    In parametric estimation, our search space is a finite-dimensional parameter space (\(\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^D\)). But in non-parametric estimation, our target is an unknown function \(p(\vec{x})\). We are searching through a Function Space—an infinite-dimensional vector space where the "vectors" themselves are continuous functions.

    Specifically, Kernel Density Estimation operates inside a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), denoted as \(\mathcal{H}\).

    A Hilbert Space is a complete vector space equipped with an inner product \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\), allowing us to measure angles, lengths, and orthogonality between functions. In this functional landscape:

    • Every training data point \(\vec{x}_i\) acts as a localized evaluation operator.
    • The smooth kernel function \(\phi(\cdot)\) satisfies Mercer’s Theorem, meaning it implicitly computes an inner product of feature mappings in an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space:
    \[K(\vec{x}, \vec{x}_i) = \langle \Phi(\vec{x}), \,\, \Phi(\vec{x}_i) \rangle_{\mathcal{H}}\]

    When you calculate a Parzen Window estimate, you are taking a collection of independent basis functions \(K(\vec{x}, \vec{x}_i)\) centered at each data point and performing a linear expansion to approximate an unknown element within that continuous function space.

    3. Smooth Kernels as an Approximation of Identity

    If you recall your functional analysis or advanced calculus courses, you likely ran into the concept of a Dirac Delta function (\(\delta(x)\)) and the Approximation of Identity.

    An ideal, infinite-data non-parametric estimator would place a perfect Dirac delta spike over every single observed data point. If a query point lands exactly on a training sample, the density would spike to infinity; if it misses by a millimeter, it drops to zero.

    Mathematically, that is a useless, non-differentiable mess. To fix it, we use the bandwidth hyperparameter \(h\) to soften the delta functions into smooth bell curves.

    \[\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h^D} \phi\left(\frac{\vec{x}}{h}\right) = \delta(\vec{x})\]

    The smoothing bandwidth \(h\) acts exactly as a scaling parameter for an Approximation of Identity. By tuning \(h\), you control how rapidly the smooth functional approximation converges toward a strict delta measure as your sample size \(N\) approaches infinity.

    The Abstract Alignment

    Statistical Language Functional Analysis Language
    Data Features (\(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\)) Coordinates sitting on a localized Metric Space.
    The Smoothing Bandwidth (\(h\)) The scaling parameter for an Approximation of Identity.
    The Kernel Function (\(\phi(\cdot)\)) A positive-definite basis function generating a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (\(\mathcal{H}\)).
    KDE Evaluation (\(\hat{p}(\vec{x})\)) Constructing a continuous function approximation via a linear combination of infinite-dimensional basis vectors.

    You saw right through the statistics wrapper. The reason non-parametric estimation can fit any arbitrary, chaotic shape in the universe is precisely because it leverages the infinite degrees of freedom granted by functional vector spaces.

    Since you've taken functional analysis, does seeing the kernel bandwidth \(h\) as an "approximation of identity" help you visualize how a bandwidth that is too large completely flattens out the fine geometric structures of your function space, while a bandwidth too small causes the space to disintegrate into noisy, isolated spikes?
