    More on \(P(X)\)

    When you sit down to code, you realize a glaring physical paradox: For a continuous, high-dimensional random variable (like a \(512 \times 512\) X-ray image vector \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{262,144}\)), the exact probability of observing any single, specific image is mathematically zero (\(P(X = x) = 0\)).

    So what are we actually calculating when we talk about \(P(X)\) in the real world, and how does a machine compute it without getting trapped in infinite dimensions?

    1. Density vs. Probability

    When we write \(P(X)\) for continuous data, we do not mean a discrete probability mass. We mean the Probability Density Function (PDF), formally written as \(f_X(x)\).

    We cannot calculate the probability of an exact pixel configuration. Instead, we calculate the density of that configuration. If you want a real probability, you have to look at the likelihood of an image falling within a tiny, microscopic hyper-volume (\(dx\)) surrounding that image vector:

    \[P(x \leq X \leq x + dx) \approx f_X(x)dx\]

    Therefore, computing \(P(X)\) means building a mathematical function that takes an image vector as an input and outputs a single scalar density value. If the output is high, that pixel arrangement looks like a realistic image from our universe. If it is low (or near zero), the pixel arrangement looks like random, impossible static noise.

    2. Approach 1: The Classical Parametric Way (Gaussian Mixture Models)

    The most direct way to calculate \(P(X)\) without knowing the infinite secrets of the universe is to make a structural assumption about how the data behaves based on its labels (\(Y\)).

    By the Law of Total Probability, we can break \(P(X)\) down using a known classification label (e.g., \(Y=0\) for healthy, \(Y=1\) for diseased):

    \[f_X(x) = f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid 0)P(Y=0) + f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid 1)P(Y=1)\]

    We can easily calculate \(P(Y=0)\) and \(P(Y=1)\) by counting the proportion of healthy vs. diseased images in our database. But how do we compute the class-conditional densities \(f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y)\)? We assume they follow a Multivariate Gaussian Distribution.

    Real-World Action: A 2-Pixel X-Ray Calculation

    To see the numbers in action, let's drastically scale down our world. Suppose our X-ray machine is a toy sensor that only captures 2 pixels (\(d=2\)). An image is a vector \(\vec{x} = [x_1, x_2]^T\).

    We analyze a historic database of hospital scans and calculate the historical mean vectors (\(\vec{\mu}\)) and covariance matrices (\(\Sigma\)) for both classes:

    • Healthy (\(Y=0\)): $\(\vec{\mu}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 120 \\ 120 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \Sigma_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 100 & 0 \\ 0 & 100 \end{bmatrix}\)$

    (Healthy lungs have balanced, predictable pixel intensities around 120). * Diseased (\(Y=1\)): $\(\vec{\mu}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 200 \\ 180 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \Sigma_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 400 & 0 \\ 0 & 400 \end{bmatrix}\)$

    (Diseased lungs show bright, highly variable scar tissue). * Class Base Rates: \(P(Y=0) = 0.70\), \(P(Y=1) = 0.30\).

    Now, a new patient walks in. The sensor scans them and spits out a novel data vector: \(\vec{x}_{\text{new}} = [140, 130]^T\). Let's calculate the exact raw value of \(P(X = \vec{x}_{\text{new}})\).

    The mathematical formula for a multivariate Gaussian density is:

    \[f_{X \mid Y}(\vec{x} \mid y) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\Sigma_y|^{1/2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2} (\vec{x} - \vec{\mu}_y)^T \Sigma_y^{-1} (\vec{x} - \vec{\mu}_y) \right)\]

    Step A: Calculate Density for the Healthy Profile (\(Y=0\))

    1. Compute the deviation:
    \[\vec{x} - \vec{\mu}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} 140-120 \\ 130-120 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 20 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix}\]
    1. Compute the Mahalanobis distance exponent:
    \[-\frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} 20 & 10 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.01 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.01 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 20 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} = -\frac{1}{2}(4 + 1) = -2.5\]
    1. Evaluate the scalar density:
    \[f_{X \mid Y}(\vec{x}_{\text{new}} \mid 0) = \frac{1}{2\pi \cdot 100} e^{-2.5} \approx (0.00159) \cdot (0.08208) = 0.0001305\]

    Step B: Calculate Density for the Diseased Profile (\(Y=1\))

    Executing the exact same steps using \(\vec{\mu}_1\) and \(\Sigma_1\):

    1. Deviation: \([140-200, 130-180]^T = [-60, -50]^T\).
    2. Exponent: \(-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{3600}{400} + \frac{2500}{400} \right) = -7.625\).
    3. Scalar density:
    \[f_{X \mid Y}(\vec{x}_{\text{new}} \mid 1) = \frac{1}{2\pi \cdot 400} e^{-7.625} \approx (0.000398) \cdot (0.000488) = 0.000000194\]

    Step C: Synthesize the Global Marginal Density \(P(X)\)

    Now, we pool the components together:

    \[f_X(\vec{x}_{\text{new}}) = (0.0001305 \cdot 0.70) + (0.000000194 \cdot 0.30)\]
    \[f_X(\vec{x}_{\text{new}}) = 0.00009135 + 0.0000000582 = 0.000091408\]

    Why this is useful:

    Look closely at the final addition step. The healthy component (\(0.00009135\)) completely dominates the diseased component (\(0.0000000582\)). This tells our AI system that this new image vector is highly typical of the healthy population and represents a very standard, normal biological variation.

    3. Approach 2: The Non-Parametric Way (Kernel Density Estimation)

    What if you refuse to assume your data follows a clean Gaussian bell curve? If your data forms complex, twisting shapes in space, parametric formulas fail. Instead, you can calculate \(P(X)\) using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE).

    Under KDE, you do not use abstract formulas. Your dataset is the formula. You place a localized miniature Gaussian distribution (a kernel) directly on top of every single training sample inside your database folder.

    To calculate \(P(X)\) for a brand-new incoming image vector \(\vec{x}\), you measure the standard Euclidean distance from your new image to every single historical image in your repository:

    \[f_X(\vec{x}) = \frac{1}{N \cdot h^d} \sum_{i=1}^{N} K\left( \frac{\|\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i\|}{h} \right)\]

    Where \(h\) is a smoothing bandwidth parameter and \(K\) is a distance scaling function.

    The Intuitive Mechanism:

    If your new X-ray image looks highly unique or anomalous, its geometric distance \(\|\vec{x} - \vec{x}_i\|\) to your existing data points will be massive. The kernel functions will drop to zero, yielding a total marginal calculation of \(P(X) \approx 0\).

    If the image looks typical, it will fall into a dense cluster of existing data points, the kernel outputs will spike, and the calculation will yield a high density score.

    4. Approach 3: The Deep Learning Way (Normalizing Flows) ( we will be dealing with this later down the course )

    While GMMs and KDE work flawlessly for low-dimensional vectors (like 2 pixels or 5 features), they completely break down when scaled to real \(512 \times 512\) X-rays due to the Curse of Dimensionality. In a quarter-million-dimensional space, all data vectors sit infinitely far apart from each other, causing KDE and Gaussians to flatline to absolute zero everywhere.

    To calculate an exact, rigorous value for \(P(X)\) on complex, high-dimensional inputs, modern machine learning relies on a class of generative networks called Normalizing Flows.

      [Real Image Space: X]        Invertible Neural Network (f)        [Latent Space: Z]
  Highly complex, intertwined  ----------------------------->  Perfect, simple, isotropic
     density layout (P(X))     <-----------------------------     Gaussian layout (P(Z))
                                   Inverse Mapping (f^-1)

    Normalizing Flows train an invertible, bijective neural network function \(f\) that maps your complex, messy image space \(\mathbb{R}^d\) down into a clean, simple, low-dimensional latent space \(\mathbb{R}^z\) governed by a standard isotropic Gaussian distribution.

    Because the neural mapping is strictly invertible (\(\vec{z} = f(\vec{x})\) and \(\vec{x} = f^{-1}(\vec{z})\)), we can invoke the rigorous geometric Change of Variables Theorem from multivariable calculus to calculate \(P(X)\) explicitly:

    \[f_X(\vec{x}) = f_Z(f(\vec{x})) \cdot \left| \det \frac{\partial f(\vec{x})}{\partial \vec{x}} \right|\]

    How the machine executes this:

    1. You feed a complex real-world X-ray image vector \(\vec{x}\) into the network.
    2. The network squeezes and transforms the image into a simple latent vector \(\vec{z}\).
    3. The machine calculates the density of that latent vector easily using a basic scalar Gaussian equation (\(f_Z\)).
    4. The machine multiplies that scalar by the determinant of the network's Jacobian Matrix \(\left| \det \frac{\partial f(\vec{x})}{\partial \vec{x}} \right|\), which acts as a geometric correction factor tracking how much the neural network stretched or compressed the space during transformation.

    The result is a highly precise, mathematically rigorous, and fully calculable density score for a massive, complex image asset.
