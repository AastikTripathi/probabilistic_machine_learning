    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 27: Aligned Probabilistic Duality: Regularized ERM as Maximum A Posteriori Estimation and Alternative Regularization Paradigms

    27.1 Why Constrained Optimization Induces Structural Bias

    In Chapter 26, we demonstrated that adding a parameter penalty function (\(\lambda \Omega(\vec{\theta})\)) to an empirical loss landscape effectively transforms an unconstrained optimization problem into a constrained exploration inside a restricted hypervolume. We must now answer the foundational question raised by the professor: Why does restricting our parameter choices systematically increase the model's internal structural bias?

    Recall our formal definition of Bias derived in Chapter 25:

    \[\text{Bias}^2 = \mathbb{E}_{X}\left[ \left( \bar{h}(X) - h^*(X) \right)^2 \right]\]

    Where \(h^*(x)\) is the absolute ideal true minimizer of the universal risk, and \(\bar{h}(x)\) is the average hypothesis generated by our algorithm when integrated across all possible training datasets.

    When you run an unconstrained Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) loop, your parameters \(\vec{\theta}\) are completely free to navigate the real coordinate space \(\mathbb{R}^P\) to fit the training points perfectly, allowing \(\bar{h}(x)\) to get as close to \(h^*(x)\) as the structural limits of the hypothesis class allow.

      [ Unconstrained Parameter Space: ℝᴾ ] 
          Unrestricted movement ───> Reaches ideal empirical minimum (Lowest Bias)

  [ Constrained Parameter Space: Ω(θ) ≤ K ]
          Forced coordinate pullback ───> Shifts average function away from truth (Higher Bias)

    The moment you introduce a constraint boundary (\(\Omega(\vec{\theta}) \leq K\)), you actively block the optimization engine from traveling to extreme coordinate vectors. If the ideal empirical minimum sits far out in space, the constraint pulls the parameters backward toward a central baseline reference point (typically the origin, \(\vec{0}\)).

    By forcing this coordinate pullback, you systematically shift the model's average functional output \(\bar{h}(x)\) away from the true, unrestricted universal target \(h^*(x)\). This architectural restriction means that regularization is, by definition, an intentional injection of structural bias. We sacrifice our model's capacity to fit the training nodes perfectly to stabilize its behavior across unseen spaces.

    27.2 The Strict Mathematical Proof of MAP-ERM Equivalence

    The professor highlights that this deterministic method of adding parameter penalties to an ERM objective is not an arbitrary engineering trick. It is the exact mathematical twin of a probabilistic Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) Estimation framework.

    Let us prove this relationship with complete algebraic rigor under a supervised learning setup.

    Step 1: Establish the Log-Posterior Foundation

    In a Bayesian framework, our model parameters are treated as random variables governed by their own distributions. Given a training design matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) and its aligned target label vector \(\vec{Y}\), we write the parametric posterior probability density function using Bayes' theorem:

    \[p(\vec{\theta} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{Y}) = \frac{p(\vec{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{\theta}) \cdot p(\vec{\theta})}{p(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X})}\]

    To find the optimal parameter configuration \(\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^*\), we maximize this posterior distribution. Because the marginal evidence denominator \(p(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X})\) does not contain our active parameter variable \(\vec{\theta}\), it acts as a constant scaling factor and can be safely dropped from our optimization operator:

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\max_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ p(\vec{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{\theta}) \cdot p(\vec{\theta}) \right]\]

    Applying a natural logarithm converts this product string into a clean, additive log-posterior field:

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\max_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ \ln p(\vec{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{\theta}) + \ln p(\vec{\theta}) \right]\]

    Step 2: Incorporate the Data Likelihood (The Gaussian Channel)

    We assume our continuous target variables \(y_i\) are independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) observations governed by a conditional Gaussian distribution centered directly on our hypothesis function \(h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i)\), with an absolute variance parameter of \(\sigma^2\):

    \[p(\vec{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 \right)\]

    Taking the natural logarithm expands this product chain into a summation of logarithmic components:

    \[\ln p(\vec{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}, \vec{\theta}) = -\frac{N}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2\]

    Step 3: Case Study A — Enforcing a Gaussian Prior (\(L_2\) Regularization)

    Suppose we formalize our prior engineering belief over our parameter space by declaring that the coefficients \(\vec{\theta}\) follow a zero-mean, multi-axis symmetric Gaussian Prior Distribution with an internal variance baseline of \(\sigma_0^2\):

    \[p(\vec{\theta}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi\sigma_0^2)^{P/2}} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \|\vec{\theta}\|_2^2 \right)\]

    We extract the natural logarithm of this prior density function:

    \[\ln p(\vec{\theta}) = -\frac{P}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma_0^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \|\vec{\theta}\|_2^2\]

    Now, we substitute our log-likelihood expansion (Step 2) and our log-Gaussian prior expansion back into our global MAP optimization equation (Step 1):

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\max_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ -\frac{N}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 - \frac{P}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma_0^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \|\vec{\theta}\|_2^2 \right]\]

    We drop the static normalization constants that do not vary with respect to \(\vec{\theta}\):

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\max_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma_0^2} \|\vec{\theta}\|_2^2 \right]\]

    To convert this maximization objective into an exact ERM minimization routine, we invert the algebraic sign and scale the entire functional envelope by the positive factor \(2\sigma^2\):

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\min_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 + \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_0^2} \right) \|\vec{\theta}\|_2^2 \right]\]

    Look closely at this finalized probabilistic equation. It is identical to our deterministic \(L_2\) Regularized ERM formula. The abstract regularization constant \(\lambda\) has been resolved to a concrete ratio of physical variances:

    \[\lambda = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma_0^2} = \frac{\text{Data Noise Variance}}{\text{Prior Parameter Variance}}\]

    This proves that running an \(L_2\) parameter penalty (Ridge Regression) is the mathematically exact equivalent of executing a Bayesian MAP estimation loop under the explicit assumption that your parameter weights follow a zero-mean Gaussian distribution.

    Step 4: Case Study B — Enforcing a Laplacian Prior (\(L_1\) Regularization)

    Now, let us alter our baseline prior assumption. Suppose we choose to model our parameters using a zero-mean continuous Laplacian Prior Distribution with a scale parameter of \(b\):

    \[p(\vec{\theta}) = \prod_{j=1}^{P} \frac{1}{2b} \exp\left( -\frac{|\theta_j|}{b} \right) = \frac{1}{(2b)^P} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{b} \sum_{j=1}^{P} |\theta_j| \right) = \frac{1}{(2b)^P} \exp\left( -\frac{\|\vec{\theta}\|_1}{b} \right)\]

    We extract the natural logarithm of this Laplacian functional:

    \[\ln p(\vec{\theta}) = -P\ln(2b) - \frac{1}{b}\|\vec{\theta}\|_1\]

    Substituting this log-prior along with our standard Gaussian log-likelihood back into our global MAP framework yields:

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\max_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 - \frac{1}{b}\|\vec{\theta}\|_1 \right]\]

    Inverting the signs and scaling the objective function by \(2\sigma^2\) isolates our final minimization criteria:

    \[\vec{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}^* = \arg\min_{\vec{\theta}} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( y_i - h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}_i) \right)^2 + \left( \frac{2\sigma^2}{b} \right) \|\vec{\theta}\|_1 \right]\]

    This completes our second proof. Running an \(L_1\) parameter penalty (Lasso) is the mathematically exact equivalent of executing a Bayesian MAP estimation loop under the explicit assumption that your parameters follow a zero-mean Laplacian distribution, where the regularization constant resolves to \(\lambda = \frac{2\sigma^2}{b}\).

    27.3 Probabilistic vs. Geometric Interpretations of Sparsity

    The professor leverages these twin derivations to reconcile two different explanations for why \(L_1\) regularization induces model sparsity much more aggressively than \(L_2\).

    1. The Probabilistic Viewpoint (The Laplacian Peak)

    If you plot a zero-mean Laplacian density curve alongside a standard normal Gaussian curve, the structural difference at the origin is stark. The Gaussian curve forms a smooth, rounded dome at its center.

    The Laplacian distribution, however, forms a sharp, steep spike right at zero (\(0.0\)). This means that a Laplacian prior packs a massive amount of its probability mass directly onto zero. When you optimize under this prior, the model is exposed to an aggressive probabilistic force that drives parameters completely down to zero unless the empirical data evidence is strong enough to pull them away.

    2. The Geometric Viewpoint (The Rhombus Vertices)

    If you translate this optimization into a constrained geometric space, your parameters are restricted to navigate inside an \(L_1\) Rhombus (or cross-polytope in higher dimensions) instead of a smooth \(L_2\) circle.

    When the expanding contours of your data loss function collide with this diamond boundary, the laws of geometry dictate that the first point of contact is highly likely to occur at one of the sharp vertices (corners). Because these vertices sit directly on the coordinate axes by construction, the intersecting coordinate naturally locks one or more feature dimensions to exactly zero (\(0.0\)). This geometric intersection performs automated feature selection.

    27.4 Optimization-Driven Regularization: Early Stopping

    The professor expands our understanding of regularization by demonstrating that we do not need to alter our objective loss function to inject structural bias. We can enforce regularized constraints purely by changing how we run our numerical optimization algorithms.

    Suppose we train an overparameterized model using standard first-order Gradient Descent. We initialize our parameter vector at the origin (\(\vec{\theta}^{(0)} = \vec{0}\)) and track our updates over time. Every single iteration step \(t\) computes a new parameter configuration, which means every single clock tick of gradient descent yields a completely unique hypothesis model.

    As the optimization path marches forward, the model gradually increases its capacity to fit complex variations in the data. If we let the algorithm run until complete numerical convergence (\(t \to \infty\)), the parameters will navigate all the way to the unconstrained empirical minimum, causing the model to overfit.

    The Checkpointing and Early Stopping Protocol

    To prevent this, we deploy an infrastructure guardrail known as Early Stopping. We track our error metrics across both our training data split and our validation data split simultaneously at every step:

    As training progresses:

    1. The Training Error decreases continuously as the model fits the training coordinates.
    2. The Validation Error decreases initially, reaches a minimum point, and then begins to curve upward—marking the exact boundary where the model abandons global trends and begins to memorize localized training noise.

    Using a technique called Checkpointing, we save our parameter states at every epoch. The moment the validation error begins to rise, we halt the training loop and roll our parameters back to the checkpoint vector that achieved the lowest validation error.

    The Mathematical Equivalence to \(L_2\) Decay

    The professor delivers an essential theoretical punchline: Halting gradient descent early at a specific step \(\tau_0\) is mathematically equivalent to running an unconstrained optimization loop to full completion under an explicit \(L_2\) parameter penalty.

    Because we initialize our weights at \(\vec{0}\) and limit our total training duration to a finite number of steps, the parameters are physically restricted from traveling past a maximum distance boundary. The optimization step budget (\(\tau_0\)) functions as an implicit radius constraint, bounding the parameter vector inside a safe hypervolume exactly like an \(L_2\) norm threshold.

    27.5 Architectural Induction: Models as Implicit Filters

    Finally, the professor generalizes the definition of regularization to its absolute limit: Every single architectural choice you make when designing a machine learning model is an implicit regularizer.

    When you choose a specific hypothesis class \(\mathcal{H}\), you are selecting a tiny subset of functions out of the infinite space of all possible mathematical relations.

    • If you restrict your model to the Linear Family (\(h(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\)), you are applying a massive structural filter. This choice completely blocks the model from learning non-linear variations, which explains why linear models are prone to underfitting—they are heavily regularized by design.
    • As we advance through the course, we will demonstrate that a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is simply a standard dense neural network that has been heavily regularized by forcing its weights to follow strict spatial translation symmetries.

    The master engineering strategy of modern machine learning is to avoid applying restrictive structural filters to your architecture from day one. Instead, you design a highly expressive, overparameterized hypothesis class (like a deep neural network or a high-degree polynomial expansion) to minimize structural bias. You then use parameter norm penalties (\(\text{L}_1/\text{L}_2\)) and optimization constraints (Early Stopping) to tune the model's variance, sliding it smoothly along the generalization curve until it reaches peak performance.

    Textbook Notes Study Guide

    The Generalist's Unifying Rules: 1. The MAP-Regularization Duality: Adding a parameter penalty function to an ERM objective is the exact mathematical equivalent of running a Bayesian MAP optimization loop. An \(L_2\) penalty maps to a zero-mean Gaussian Prior, while an \(L_1\) penalty maps to a zero-mean Laplacian Prior. 2. The Structural Sparsity Law: \(L_1\) regularization induces sparse parameter configurations because its Laplacian prior concentrates massive probability mass right at zero, and its geometric cross-polytope boundary forces optimization intersections to land directly on the coordinate axes. 3. The Early Stopping Radius: Halting numerical optimization early using a validation dataset tracking loop is mathematically equivalent to running an \(L_2\) weight decay model to full convergence. Your step allocation acts as an implicit radius constraint that keeps parameters safely bounded around the origin.
