    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 19: Generalized Linear Models, Basis Transformations, and the Evolution of Feature Representation

    19.1 The Structural Limitation of Naive Linearity

    In the previous chapter, we derived the closed-form solution for Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) under a naive linear assumption. We defined our hypothesis mapping function as a strict weighted combination of the raw input coordinates:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x} = \theta_0 + \theta_1 x_1 + \theta_2 x_2 + \dots + \theta_D x_D\]

    While this framework yields an elegant, direct matrix inversion via the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse, it faces a major physical constraint: the real universe is profoundly non-linear. If the true relationship between your input features and your targets curves quadratically, cubically, or sinusoidally, a naive linear model will suffer from severe structural bias. No matter how cleanly you invert your data matrices, a straight line cannot accurately fit a bending curve.

          [ Raw Feature Space: ℝᴰ ]                     [ Transformed Feature Space: ℝᴾ ]
       Non-linear trajectories                        Perfect linear alignment
       Impossible to fit with a hyperplane.           Solvable via OLS Normal Equations.
                 |                                              ^
                 |                                              |
                 ------------------[ Φ(x) Transformation ]-------

    To resolve this limitation without sacrificing the mathematical cleanliness of linear algebra, we introduce Generalized Linear Models via basis transformations. We demonstrate that we can model highly complex, twisting geometric curves while completely preserving our linear optimization pipeline.

    19.2 The Feature Transformation Mapping (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\))

    The core operational trick of a generalized linear model is to separate the data space from the parameter space. Instead of forcing our model to be linear with respect to our raw features \(\vec{x}\), we apply a fixed, precomputed non-linear transformation function—denoted as \(\vec{\phi}(\cdot)\)—to project our data into a completely different coordinate space before optimization begins.

    Definition 19.1 (The Feature Transformation Mapping). Let \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\) represent a raw input vector in a \(D\)-dimensional data space. We define \(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\) as a fixed, user-defined non-linear mapping function that projects our raw coordinates into a \(P\)-dimensional feature space: $\(\vec{\phi}: \mathbb{R}^D \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^P\)$

    \[\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}) = \left[ \phi_1(\vec{x}), \,\, \phi_2(\vec{x}), \dots, \phi_P(\vec{x}) \right]^T\]

    Under this layout, each individual coordinate dimension \(\phi_j(\vec{x})\) in our new space can be a complex function of one or more dimensions of our original data:

    • Single-Dimension Transformations: \(\phi_1(\vec{x}) = (x_1)^2\) or \(\phi_2(\vec{x}) = (x_2)^3\)
    • Cross-Dimensional Interactions: \(\phi_3(\vec{x}) = (x_1)^2 \cdot (x_2)^6\)

    By pulling our raw data through this mapping matrix, our hypothesis function transforms into:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}) = \theta_1 \phi_1(\vec{x}) + \theta_2 \phi_2(\vec{x}) + \dots + \theta_P \phi_P(\vec{x})\]

    The Core Realization of Linearity

    Look closely at this new hypothesis equation. This function is undeniably non-linear with respect to the raw data space \(\mathbb{R}^D\). However, it remains perfectly linear with respect to the parameter vector \(\vec{\theta}\) inside the transformed feature space \(\mathbb{R}^P\).

    Because the parameters interact with our transformed features through a simple inner product, our entire underlying optimization pipeline remains unchanged. We are still performing linear regression; we have simply changed the coordinates of the canvas we are drawing on.

    19.3 The Matrix Formulation of Generalized Linear Regression

    Because our optimization remains linear in the parameter space \(\mathbb{R}^P\), we can deploy the exact same closed-form matrix calculus we derived for Ordinary Least Squares in Chapter 18.

    Step 1: Construct the Transformed Design Matrix

    We pass our entire training dataset of \(N\) rows through our fixed transformation function \(\vec{\phi}(\cdot)\). We stack the resulting \(P\)-dimensional row vectors horizontally to construct our new transformed Design Matrix, denoted as \(\mathbf{\Phi}\):

    \[\mathbf{\Phi} = \begin{bmatrix} \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}_1)^T \\ \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}_2)^T \\ \vdots \\ \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}_N)^T \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_1(\vec{x}_1) & \phi_2(\vec{x}_1) & \dots & \phi_P(\vec{x}_1) \\ \phi_1(\vec{x}_2) & \phi_2(\vec{x}_2) & \dots & \phi_P(\vec{x}_2) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \phi_1(\vec{x}_N) & \phi_2(\vec{x}_N) & \dots & \phi_P(\vec{x}_N) \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times P}\]

    Step 2: Resolve the Normal Equations

    We substitute this transformed design matrix directly into our Empirical Risk Minimization objective function using a standard squared error loss metric:

    \[\vec{\theta}^* = \arg\min_{\vec{\theta}} \frac{1}{N} \|\mathbf{\Phi}\vec{\theta} - \vec{Y}\|_2^2\]

    Computing the vector gradient with respect to \(\vec{\theta}\) and setting the result to zero yields the exact same system of Normal Equations, adapted to our \(P\)-dimensional feature space:

    \[\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} \vec{\theta}^* = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \vec{Y}\]

    Assuming our transformed matrix product possesses full column rank, we isolate our optimal parameters using the exact same Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse solution:

    \[\vec{\theta}^* = \left( \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \vec{Y}\]

    19.4 Classical Instantiations: Polynomial Regression and Fourier Series

    To see the flexibility of this framework, let us examine two classical applications where non-linear curves are linearized using fixed transformations.

    1. Polynomial Regression

    Consider a simple one-dimensional data environment where \(x \in \mathbb{R}^1\) and \(y \in \mathbb{R}^1\). We choose a fixed polynomial kernel to map our data into a 3-dimensional feature space (\(P = 3\)):

    \[\vec{\phi}(x) = \left[ 1, \,\, x, \,\, x^2 \right]^T\]

    Our generalized linear hypothesis expands to:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(x) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{\phi}(x) = \theta_0 + \theta_1 x + \theta_2 x^2\]

    This creates a bending quadratic parabola in your raw data plots. Yet, because the parameters \(\theta_j\) are separated by addition, a computer can solve for the perfect curved fit instantly using basic matrix inversion.

    2. Fourier Series Expansion

    Suppose you are analyzing periodic or wave-based data patterns. You can define your fixed transformation function \(\vec{\phi}(\cdot)\) to be a collection of sine and cosine wave functions:

    \[\vec{\phi}(x) = \left[ 1, \,\, \sin(x), \,\, \cos(x), \,\, \sin(2x), \,\, \cos(2x), \dots \right]^T\]

    Optimizing this system using our OLS normal equations isolates the exact coefficients needed to reconstruct complex periodic trajectories. A classical Fourier series is simply an empirical risk minimization routine executing over a fixed sinusoidal coordinate manifold.

    19.5 The Probabilistic Correspondence: Heteroscedastic Gaussian Duality

    The professor bridges our structural framework back to the rules of probability theory by proving that choosing an architectural kernel in the ERM world matches an explicit density assumption in the probabilistic world.

    Consider a binary classification problem (\(Y \in \{0, 1\}\)) where we build a Bayes Optimal Classifier by evaluating the posterior ratio of two continuous class-conditional densities, assuming both follow Multivariate Gaussian profiles:

    \[p(\vec{x} \mid Y = 0) = \mathcal{N}\left(\vec{x}; \,\, \vec{\mu}_0, \,\, \Sigma_0\right) \quad \text{and} \quad p(\vec{x} \mid Y = 1) = \mathcal{N}\left(\vec{x}; \,\, \vec{\mu}_1, \,\, \Sigma_1\right)\]

    When we evaluate the decision boundary where the posterior probabilities balance perfectly, our algebraic equations record the difference between these two continuous quadratic forms.

    Case Statistical Covariance Parameter Geometric Shape of Decision Boundary
    Homoscedastic Setup Identical Covariance Matrices: \(\Sigma_0 = \Sigma_1\) Linear Hyperplane. The quadratic matrix terms (\(x^2\)) cancel out completely during subtraction, leaving a pure linear function of data.
    Heteroscedastic Setup Different Covariance Matrices: \(\Sigma_0 \neq \Sigma_1\) Quadratic Surface. The quadratic matrix terms survive the subtraction, forcing the decision boundary to warp into a curve.

    This exposes the grand duality: If you choose to run a Generalized Linear Model with a quadratic feature transformation (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}) = [x, x^2]^T\)), you are implicitly making a foundational assumption that your class-conditional distributions are heteroscedastic Gaussians possessing entirely different spatial variances (\(\Sigma_0 \neq \Sigma_1\)).

    19.6 The Historical Evolution: The Leap to Deep Learning

    To close the lecture, the professor steps back to look at the history of machine learning engineering, outlining the core transition that separates the classical era from modern deep learning.

           [ The Classical Era: Manual Engineering ]              [ The Modern Era: Learnable Representations ]
        Humans manually design a fixed feature vector Φ.      The transformation weights W are left free.
        Examples: SIFT, Gabor Filters, Wavelets.             Optimized directly via backpropagation.
                 |                                                      |
                 v                                                      v
        Limited by human domain insight.                     Universal feature representation.

    In the pre-deep learning era, engineers never passed raw data vectors directly into their classifiers. If you wanted to analyze a speech signal or a digital image, you spent years designing a specialized feature transformation function \(\vec{\phi}(\cdot)\) by hand.

    Scientists dedicated their entire careers to discovering robust hand-crafted feature extractors—resulting in famous historical transformations named after their creators, such as Gabor Filters, Fourier Transforms, SIFT features, and Wavelet expansions.

    The professor notes that this manual approach is inherently limited by human insight. We are forcing our data to pass through a rigid, human-designed bottleneck.

    The Modern Neural Breakthrough

    Modern deep learning completely flips this paradigm. Instead of forcing a human to engineer a fixed transformation function \(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\), we treat the feature mapping components themselves as a massive collection of learnable parameter weights (\(\mathbf{W}\)) left completely free inside the network:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \,\, \mathbf{W})\]

    Instead of fixing the canvas, a neural network uses empirical risk minimization to optimize both the final linear decision weights \(\vec{\theta}\) and the internal feature representation layers \(\mathbf{W}\) simultaneously. The network looks at the raw data and automatically figures out how to warp, stretch, and transform the space until the categories become cleanly and linearly separable.

    Textbook Notes Study Guide

    The Generalist's Theoretical Map: 1. Generalized Linear Regression projects a raw data vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\) into a non-linear feature space \(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^P\). While the resulting model curves over our raw inputs, it remains perfectly linear with respect to its parameters \(\vec{\theta}\), allowing us to solve it using our standard OLS Normal Equations: \(\vec{\theta}^* = \left( \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{\Phi} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{\Phi}^T \vec{Y}\). 2. The Covariance Duality: Slicing a space with a quadratic basis expansion is the exact mathematical equivalent of building a probabilistic Bayes classifier under the assumption that your classes follow Gaussian distributions with unequal covariances (\(\Sigma_0 \neq \Sigma_1\)). 3. The Representation Shift: Deep learning eliminates the need for manual, hand-crafted feature engineering (like SIFT or wavelets). A neural network uses optimization to learn the optimal feature transformation mapping (\(\vec{\phi}\)) directly from raw data fields.

    Our exploration of generalized linear expansions is complete. We have mapped out basis transformations, derived the Normal Equations over feature spaces, and established how neural networks automate this entire process.

    As we prepare for the next lecture to dive explicitly into the empirical risk minimization mechanics of Classification Linearity, how does your architectural understanding of basis expansions help you visualize how a neural network's hidden layers reshape a chaotic cluster of raw coordinates into a clean, linearly separable manifold?

    To see exactly how this modern breakthrough operates, it helps to decouple the math into standard neural network architecture.

    In the equation \(h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \,\, \mathbf{W})\):

    • \(\mathbf{W}\) represents the weights of all the hidden layers (the learnable feature extractor backbone).
    • \(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \,\, \mathbf{W})\) is the representation vector output by the final hidden layer.
    • \(\vec{\theta}^T\) represents the weights of the output layer (the standard linear classifier or regressor sitting on top).

    Here is how this looks in action across different domains, contrasting the classical "fixed" approach with the modern "learnable" breakthrough.

    Concrete Examples in Modern Architectures

    1. Computer Vision (Image Classification)

    • The Classical Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\) is fixed): To classify an image of a lung X-ray, engineers manually wrote algorithms like SIFT or Gabor filters to extract edges, shadows, and textures. This turned the raw image pixels \(\vec{x}\) into a fixed feature vector. A linear Support Vector Machine (\(\vec{\theta}\)) was then trained on those static features. If the hand-crafted math missed a texture pattern, the model failed.
    • The Deep Learning Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \mathbf{W})\) is learned): You feed raw, un-engineered pixel arrays directly into a Convolutional Neural Network (like a ResNet).
    • The convolutional kernels (\(\mathbf{W}\)) start with completely random values.
    • As the network trains via Empirical Risk Minimization, the early layers automatically figure out how to become edge detectors, and deeper layers combine those edges into complex shapes specifically optimized to spot anomalies in your dataset.
    • The very last layer is just a simple linear layer (\(\vec{\theta}^T\)) drawing a straight hyperplane through the highly optimized features that the hidden layers learned to extract.

    2. Natural Language Processing (Text Ingestion)

    • The Classical Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\) is fixed): Sentences were passed through fixed mathematical frequency counters like TF-IDF or Bag-of-Words. The text was transformed into a sparse matrix mapping token counts, which was then fed into a linear Logistic Regression model. The model had no native grasp of syntax, word order, or context.
    • The Deep Learning Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \mathbf{W})\) is learned): Raw text token indices are fed into a Transformer network (like the backbone of an LLM).
    • The Multi-Head Self-Attention matrices (\(\mathbf{W}\)) are left entirely free.
    • Through training on text bodies, these weights automatically learn how to project raw words into a dense continuous space where words with similar semantic meanings sit close together, and attention heads dynamically adjust based on context.
    • The final classification head (\(\vec{\theta}^T\)) simply runs a linear dot-product over this learned contextual embedding space to calculate the next-token probability.

    3. Tabular Engineering (Complex Price Prediction)

    • The Classical Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x})\) is fixed): If you wanted to predict a house's value based on raw inputs like Latitude and Longitude, a linear model would fail because geographic boundaries curve non-linearly. Engineers had to manually write cross-features (e.g., Latitude × Longitude or binning coordinates into localized polynomial zones) to force linear alignment.
    • The Deep Learning Way (\(\vec{\phi}(\vec{x}; \mathbf{W})\) is learned): You feed the raw coordinates directly into a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) with dense layers and non-linear activations (like ReLU). The hidden layer weights (\(\mathbf{W}\)) compress, warp, and bend the coordinate grid internally, mapping it to a latent feature space where geographic pricing trends line up linearly for the final output layer (\(\vec{\theta}\)) to evaluate.

    1. Current Phase: Non-linear modeling via fixed, precomputed basis transformations (Polynomial regression, Fourier series expansions, hand-crafted kernels).

    4. The Culmination Phase: Transitioning into Neural Networks, where we officially unlock \(\mathbf{W}\) and let the model optimize its own feature transformations directly from raw data fields.
