    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 30: The Primal Lagrangian Formulation, Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) Conditions, and the Foundations of Wolfe Duality in Support Vector Machines

    30.1 The Primal Constrained Optimization Structure

    In Chapter 29, we derived the geometric foundations of the Hard-Margin Support Vector Machine (SVM). We proved that maximizing the physical corridor width separating our parallel canonical boundary hyperplanes (\(\frac{2}{\|\vec{w}\|_2}\)) requires solving a standard convex optimization problem bounded by linear inequality constraints:

    \[\min_{\vec{w}, b} \frac{1}{2} \|\vec{w}\|_2^2 \quad \text{subject to} \quad y_i \left( \vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i + b \right) \geq 1 \quad \forall \,\, i = 1, 2, \dots, N\]

    The professor highlights a profound structural inversion that occurs when we transition from standard Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) algorithms to this max-margin setup:

    • In Classical ERM: The objective function minimizes a loss computed over the data rows, while the regularization constraint is a function of the parameter weights (\(\Omega(\vec{\theta}) \leq K\)).
    • In Max-Margin SVMs: The operational roles are swapped. The objective function is defined purely by the geometric length of the parameter weights (\(\frac{1}{2}\|\vec{w}\|_2^2\)), while the constraints are dictated entirely by the physics of the training data rows and their class labels (\(y_i(\vec{w}^T\vec{x}_i + b) \geq 1\)).

    Despite this structural inversion, the underlying optimization mechanics remain aligned. We can map this primal formulation back onto our standard regularized ERM framework under an explicit piece-wise loss metric known as the Hinge Loss Function:

    \[L_{\text{Hinge}}(z) = \max(0, \,\, 1 - z)\]

    Under this lens, an SVM is mathematically equivalent to an ERM pipeline running a linear hypothesis class over a squared \(L_2\) parameter penalty constraint, using Hinge loss to penalize any training instance that crosses into the margin corridor. Because this global optimization space is perfectly convex, we can bypass approximate first-order numerical gradient loops and solve for the absolute global parameters analytically using formal duality theory.

    30.2 Constructing the Primal Lagrangian Function

    To solve this inequality-constrained problem with absolute precision, we convert our boundaries into an unconstrained optimization functional. We rewrite our linear inequality constraints to match standard convex programming layout (\(\text{Constraint}_i \leq 0\)):

    \[1 - y_i\left( \vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i + b \right) \leq 0 \quad \forall \,\, i = 1, 2, \dots, N\]

    We allocate an independent, non-negative scalar Lagrange Multiplier component (also denoted as a dual variable) to every single training instance row stored in our matrix. Let the vector of these multipliers be denoted as \(\vec{\mu} = [\mu_1, \mu_2, \dots, \mu_N]^T \in \mathbb{R}^N\).

    We assemble our global Primal Lagrangian Function, denoted as \(\mathcal{L}(\vec{w}, b, \vec{\mu})\):

    \[\mathcal{L}(\vec{w}, b, \vec{\mu}) = \frac{1}{2} \vec{w}^T \vec{w} + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i \left[ 1 - y_i \left( \vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i + b \right) \right]\]

    Literal Variable and Operator Breakdown:

    • \(\mathcal{L}(\vec{w}, b, \vec{\mu})\): The scalar output value of the unified Lagrangian function, acting as a functional mapping across the primal parameter vector space and the dual multiplier vector space.
    • \(\vec{w}\): The trainable parameter weight vector of length \(D\), mapping the directional normal angle of the decision boundary.
    • \(b\): The standalone scalar intercept bias managing the hyperplane's perpendicular distance from the coordinate origin.
    • \(\vec{\mu}\): The non-negative vector of \(N\) Lagrange multipliers ($ \mu_i \geq 0$).
    • \(\frac{1}{2} \vec{w}^T \vec{w}\): The squared \(L_2\) norm of the parameter weights, acting as our core objective function to minimize the model's structural variance.
    • \(\sum_{i=1}^{N}\): The linear summation operator iterating across every independent constraint row inside the training folder from index \(i=1\) to \(i=N\).
    • \(1 - y_i(\vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i + b)\): The explicit linear distance constraint evaluating the margin compliance of data vector \(\vec{x}_i\) matching target label \(y_i\).

    30.3 The Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) Optimality Conditions

    Because our primal objective is perfectly convex and our constraints are linear, the system satisfies Slater's Condition, guaranteeing that the primal and dual solutions converge onto an identical coordinate point with zero duality gap. To isolate this unique global saddle point, our parameters must satisfy the complete system of Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) Conditions.

    1. Primal Stationarity (The Derivative Conditions)

    The partial derivatives of our Lagrangian function with respect to the primal variables \(\vec{w}\) and \(b\) must vanish completely at the global optimum.

    First, we evaluate the multi-axis vector derivative with respect to our weight parameters \(\vec{w}\):

    \[\nabla_{\vec{w}} \mathcal{L}(\vec{w}, b, \vec{\mu}) = \vec{0} \implies \nabla_{\vec{w}} \left[ \frac{1}{2} \vec{w}^T \vec{w} \right] - \nabla_{\vec{w}} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i \vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i \right] = \vec{0}\]
    \[\vec{w}^* - \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i \vec{x}_i = \vec{0} \implies \vec{w}^* = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i \vec{x}_i \quad \mathbf{(Eq. \,\, 30.1)}\]

    This provides an exceptional structural insight. At the global optimum, the ideal weight vector \(\vec{w}^*\) is entirely expressed as a linear combination of the training data rows (\(\vec{x}_i\)), scaled by their labels (\(y_i\)) and their optimal Lagrange multipliers (\(\mu_i\)).

    Next, we evaluate the scalar derivative with respect to our intercept bias \(b\):

    \[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b} = 0 \implies \frac{\partial}{\partial b} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i - \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i \vec{w}^T \vec{x}_i - \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i b \right] = 0\]
    \[-\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i = 0 \implies \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i = 0 \quad \mathbf{(Eq. \,\, 30.2)}\]

    This identity enforces a balancing constraint across our dual vector space: the sum of our optimal multipliers weighted by their respective binary labels must equal exactly zero.

    2. Primal Feasibility

    Every training instance must comply with the foundational classification safety boundaries:

    \[1 - y_i \left( (\vec{w}^*)^T \vec{x}_i + b^* \right) \leq 0 \quad \forall \,\, i = 1, 2, \dots, N\]

    3. Dual Feasibility

    Every Lagrange multiplier must remain strictly non-negative:

    \[\mu_i \geq 0 \quad \forall \,\, i = 1, 2, \dots, N\]

    4. Complementary Slackness

    The product of each Lagrange multiplier and its respective constraint function must equal exactly zero at the optimum:

    \[\mu_i \left[ 1 - y_i \left( (\vec{w}^*)^T \vec{x}_i + b^* \right) \right] = 0 \quad \forall \,\, i = 1, 2, \dots, N\]

    This condition uncovers the exact mechanics of model sparsity. For every data instance in your folder, this product can only equal zero through one of two mathematical paths:

    • Path A (\(\mu_i = 0\)): If a training data vector lands safely outside the corridor (\(y_i(\vec{w}^T\vec{x}_i + b) > 1\)), its multiplier is forced to be exactly zero. Looking back at Eq. 30.1, because \(\mu_i = 0\), this point is completely wiped out from the model's equations. It exerts zero influence over the final shape of the decision boundary.
    • Path B (\(\mu_i > 0\)): If a multiplier is strictly positive, the complementary constraint forces the bracketed term to be exactly zero: \(y_i(\vec{w}^T\vec{x}_i + b) = 1\). This means the data vector sits precisely on the edge of the margin corridor. These critical instances are our Support Vectors.

    We define the active Support Vector set \(S\) as:

    \[S \triangleq \{ \vec{x}_i \in D \mid \mu_i > 0 \}\]

    The complete decision boundary is supported exclusively by this sparse subset of boundary points. If you delete any other data rows from your database folder, the parameters of your classifier will not budge by a single decimal point.

    30.4 The Framework of Wolfe Duality

    To calculate our optimal weights, we must compute the values of our unknown Lagrange multipliers \(\vec{\mu}\). We achieve this by shifting our optimization problem from the primal domain (\(\vec{w}, b\)) into the Dual Domain via a Wolfe Duality framework.

    We construct our Dual Objective Function, denoted as \(Q(\vec{\mu})\), by calculating the infimum (the greatest lower bound) of our primal Lagrangian evaluated across our primal parameters:

    \[Q(\vec{\mu}) \triangleq \inf_{\vec{w}, b} \mathcal{L}(\vec{w}, b, \vec{\mu})\]

    The Mathematical Distinction: Infimum vs. Minimum: The professor notes a key distinction in optimization terminology. A Minimum describes a lower bound value that is explicitly reached by a specific coordinate within a set. An Infimum represents the absolute greatest lower bound of a function over a domain, regardless of whether that boundary coordinate is explicitly achieved by an active point in the space.

    Because our Lagrangian function is smooth and convex with respect to \(\vec{w}\) and \(b\), this infimum is exactly achieved at the stationarity coordinates derived in our first KKT condition.

    To construct the explicit formula for \(Q(\vec{\mu})\), we will take our stationarity equations (\(\vec{w}^* = \sum \mu_i y_i \vec{x}_i\) and \(\sum \mu_i y_i = 0\)) and substitute them directly back into our primal Lagrangian function, eliminating the primal variables entirely.

    Once \(Q(\vec{\mu})\) is isolated, we execute our global Dual Optimization Problem:

    \[\max_{\vec{\mu}} Q(\vec{\mu}) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \mu_i \geq 0 \quad \forall \,\, i=1,\dots,N \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_i y_i = 0\]

    Because the primal problem was a minimization task, its dual representation is a maximization task. Moving into this dual domain grants us a massive architectural advantage: we have eliminated our complex structural parameters and rewritten our entire classification system as a function of Lagrange multipliers bounded by simple box constraints. Once our software backend evaluates this dual function using standard quadratic solvers to find \(\vec{\mu}^*\), we can instantly map the results back to our primal space to recover our optimal decision boundary parameters (\(\vec{w}^*, b^*\)) with perfect numerical precision.

    Textbook Notes Study Guide

    The Generalist's Theoretical Map: 1. The Dual Role Swap: Support Vector Machines invert classical regularized ERM structures. The objective function optimizes parameter length (\(\frac{1}{2}\|\vec{w}\|_2^2\)) while our training data rows function as a massive system of linear inequality constraints. 2. The Primal Expansion Law: Applying the KKT stationarity condition proves that the optimal weight vector \(\vec{w}^*\) is expressed entirely as a linear combination of training data vectors: \(\vec{w}^* = \sum \mu_i y_i \vec{x}_i\). 3. The Complementary Slackness Filter: The KKT complementary slackness condition forces the Lagrange multipliers to drop to exactly zero for all instances residing safely outside the margin corridor. The decision boundary is anchored exclusively by a sparse subset of boundary coordinates known as Support Vectors (\(\mu_i > 0\)).

    Our primal Lagrangian formulation and KKT conditions are completely formalized. We have derived the stationarity vectors, isolated the sparsity filters of complementary slackness, and established the baseline requirements of Wolfe Duality.
