    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 24: Empirical Risk Minimization for Classification, Non-Linear Link Functions, and First-Order Numerical Optimization

    24.1 The Range Space Mismatch in Classification

    In the previous chapter, we established the core optimization solution for Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression. We mapped an augmented feature vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{D+1}\) to a continuous target scalar \(y \in \mathbb{R}^1\) using a simple parameter inner product:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\]

    While this linear mapping functions perfectly for regression problems, it hits a hard structural wall the moment we transition to Classification. In a binary classification problem, our target space is strictly discrete and categorical:

    \[\mathcal{Y} = \{0, \,\, 1\}\]

    If you attempt to use a raw linear hypothesis function \(h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\) for classification, you trigger a fundamental Range Space Mismatch. The range of a linear inner product is completely unbounded, spanning the entire real continuum: \((-\infty, \infty)\).

    If your model processes an extreme input vector, it might calculate an output score of \(+5,000\) or \(-342\). These values are structurally meaningless when trying to assign a discrete binary classification label. To resolve this, we must modify our hypothesis architecture. We require a mathematical mechanism that compresses the infinite real line down into a safe, bounded probability domain.

    24.2 Binary Classification and the Sigmoid Link Function

    To align our unbounded linear calculations with a discrete binary target space, we perform a functional composition. We pass our inner product \(\vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}\) through a non-linear activation envelope known as a Link Function. For binary classification, we choose the standard Logistic Sigmoid Function, denoted as \(\sigma(t)\):

    \[\sigma(t) \triangleq \frac{1}{1 + e^{-t}}\]

    Direct Technical Correction: During the lecture, the professor made a quick slip of the tongue, stating that the sigmoid curve approaches \(0\) when \(t = 0\). Let's correct that real quick: evaluating the formula at \(t=0\) yields exactly \(\frac{1}{1+1} = 0.5\). The function asymptotically approaches \(0\) as \(t \to -\infty\), and approaches \(1\) as \(t \to \infty\). This property makes it perfect for modeling probabilities.

    By composing our linear model with this sigmoid link function, we build the complete architecture for Logistic Regression:

    \[h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) = \sigma\left( \vec{\theta}^T \vec{x} \right) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\vec{\theta}^T \vec{x}}}\]

    Because the range of this function is strictly bounded on the open interval \((0, 1)\), its output value can be interpreted directly as a conditional probability mass: \(h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) \equiv P(Y = 1 \mid \vec{x})\).

    To convert this continuous probability score into a final, discrete binary decision, we apply a threshold rule using a decision hyperparameter \(\tau\):

    \[\text{Final Classifier Assignment: } \hat{y} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } h_{\vec{\theta}}(\vec{x}) \geq \tau \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

    The choice of the threshold parameter \(\tau\) determines your model's real-world performance. By adjusting \(\tau\), you slide your model along its Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve, allowing you to tune the balance between False Positives and False Negatives to match your application's safety requirements.

    24.3 Multi-Class Classification and the Softmax Operator

    When our environment scales from a binary choice to an \(M\)-class categorical sorting problem (\(Y \in \{1, 2, \dots, M\}\)), a single scalar equation can no longer track the boundaries. We must track an entire vector of scores simultaneously.

    First, we represent our discrete category labels using a One-Hot Encoding layout. If we are sorting data across \(M = 3\) target classes, the labels are mapped to orthogonal basis vectors:

    \[\text{Class 1} \to \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \text{Class 2} \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \text{Class 3} \to \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

    To predict these multi-axis targets, we replace our single parameter column with a global Parameter Matrix, denoted as \(\mathbf{\Theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times D}\). Multiplying this matrix by our feature vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^D\) outputs an \(M\)-dimensional vector of raw, unconstrained real numbers known as Logits (\(\vec{v}\)):

    \[\vec{v} = \mathbf{\Theta}\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^M\]

    To map this vector of unconstrained scores into a valid probability distribution, we deploy a multi-dimensional generalization of the sigmoid function known as the Softmax Operator.

    Definition 24.1 (The Softmax Transformation). Let \(\vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^M\) represent a vector of raw logit scores. The Softmax operator maps \(\vec{v}\) to a probability vector \(\vec{z}\) by calculating the relative exponential mass of each individual coordinate index \(j\): $\(\text{softmax}(\vec{v})_j \triangleq \frac{e^{v_j}}{\sum_{m=1}^{M} e^{v_m}}\)$

    By combining our parameter matrix multiplication with this multi-class link operator, we formulate the complete architecture for Multi-Class Logistic Regression (also known as Softmax Regression):

    \[h_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{x}) = \text{softmax}\left( \mathbf{\Theta}\vec{x} \right)\]
    \[\text{Where: } \quad \left(h_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{x})\right)_j = \frac{e^{\vec{\theta}_j^T \vec{x}}}{\sum_{m=1}^{M} e^{\vec{\theta}_m^T \vec{x}}}\]

    Every individual coordinate index \(j\) in the output vector is strictly bounded between 0 and 1, and the entire collection sums to exactly \(1.0\). This output acts as a valid probability distribution over our multi-class target simplex.

    24.4 The Analytical Breakdown: Why We Need Numerical Optimization

    Now that our classification architectures are formalized, we can set up our Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) problem using a standard vector difference metric over \(N\) training samples:

    \[\mathbf{\Theta}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{\Theta}} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left\| \text{softmax}\left(\mathbf{\Theta}\vec{x}_i\right) - \vec{y}_i \right\|_2^2\]

    This brings us to the core problem of classification optimization: this equation cannot be solved analytically.

    In Chapter 18, we solved linear regression instantly because its loss function was purely quadratic, creating a smooth, symmetric bowl with a single vector derivative that decoupled cleanly.

    However, when we introduce non-linear link functions like the Sigmoid or Softmax operators, the parameter weights become trapped inside non-linear transcendental functions. If you take the derivative of this objective function with respect to \(\mathbf{\Theta}\) and set it to zero, the parameters cannot be separated or isolated using standard matrix algebra. There is no closed-form pseudo-inverse matrix equation for logistic or softmax regression. We must abandon analytical shortcuts and transition to Numerical Optimization.

    24.5 First-Order Optimization: The Mechanics of Gradient Descent

    To solve these intractable parameter landscapes, machine learning algorithms rely on Gradient Descent.

    Instead of computing the global minimum in a single step, we seed our parameters with a random initial guess (\(\vec{\theta}^{(0)}\)) and iteratively update our coordinates by taking small steps down the local slope of the error surface.

    The Gradient Descent Protocol: Let \(\hat{R}(\vec{\theta})\) represent our empirical risk cost landscape. For optimization time steps \(t = 0, 1, 2, \dots\), we update our parameter vector using the following recursive formula: $\(\vec{\theta}^{(t+1)} = \vec{\theta}^{(t)} - \alpha \nabla_{\vec{\theta}} \hat{R}\left(\vec{\theta}^{(t)}\right)\)$

    Where \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}^1_{>0}\) is a scalar hyperparameter known as the Learning Rate.

    The Core Justification: The Taylor Series Link

    The professor notes that this iterative formula is not just an intuitive heuristic; it is derived directly from the mathematics of a Taylor Series Expansion.

    Let \(\hat{R}(\vec{\theta})\) be a smooth, continuous cost function. If we perturb our parameter coordinates by a small directional step vector \(\Delta \vec{\theta}\), we can approximate the new cost value by evaluating its first-order multi-variable Taylor series expansion around our active coordinate location:

    \[\hat{R}\left(\vec{\theta} + \Delta \vec{\theta}\right) \approx \hat{R}(\vec{\theta}) + \left( \nabla_{\vec{\theta}} \hat{R}(\vec{\theta}) \right)^T \Delta \vec{\theta}\]

    To minimize our risk (\(\hat{R}\left(\vec{\theta} + \Delta \vec{\theta}\right) < \hat{R}(\vec{\theta})\)), we need the change term to be as negative as possible. We want to maximize the downward drop:

    \[\min_{\Delta \vec{\theta}} \left( \nabla_{\vec{\theta}} \hat{R}(\vec{\theta}) \right)^T \Delta \vec{\theta}\]

    By Cauchy-Schwarz geometry, the inner product between two vectors reaches its absolute minimum when the vectors point in diametrically opposite directions. Therefore, to achieve the steepest local descent, our directional step vector \(\Delta \vec{\theta}\) must be oriented directly opposite to the local gradient vector:

    \[\Delta \vec{\theta} = -\alpha \nabla_{\vec{\theta}} \hat{R}(\vec{\theta})\]

    Substituting this step back into our parameter update equation (\(\vec{\theta}^{(t+1)} = \vec{\theta}^{(t)} + \Delta \vec{\theta}\)) yields the exact mathematical formula for Gradient Descent.

    24.6 Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss

    The professor concludes the lecture by highlighting that a squared error loss metric (\(L_2\)) is rarely used for classification tasks. Instead, we evaluate our estimation errors using the Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss Function.

    Let \(\vec{y}\) represent our true, one-hot encoded target vector of length \(M\). Let \(\vec{h} = h_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{x})\) represent the predicted probability distribution output by our softmax layer. The cross-entropy penalty is defined mathematically as:

    \[L_{\text{CE}}(\vec{y}, \,\, \vec{h}) \triangleq -\sum_{k=1}^{M} y_k \ln(h_k)\]

    Fixing the Logarithmic Edge Case

    The student at the end of the lecture notes caught a critical formatting error on the boards. The professor originally wrote the log target index flipped (\(h_k \ln y_k\)).

    Let’s ensure this is written with complete precision: The log operator must wrap the model's prediction (\(h_k\)), not the true label (\(y_k\)). Because the true target vector \(\vec{y}\) is one-hot encoded, it contains absolute zeros for all non-active categories. If the logarithm wrapped the target, calculating \(\ln(0)\) would evaluate to negative infinity (\(-\infty\)), causing your software program to crash instantly.

    By wrapping the prediction instead, the one-hot target vector acts as a clean selector mask. Because \(y_k = 0\) for all incorrect classes, those terms drop out of the summation entirely. The function isolates the single index \(c\) where the true label is active (\(y_c = 1\)), reducing the multi-class cross-entropy loss down to a single logarithmic evaluation:

    \[L_{\text{CE}}(\vec{y}, \,\, \vec{h}) = -1 \cdot \ln(h_c)\]

    This loss function penalizes errors heavily. If the model confidently predicts a low probability for the true class (\(h_c \to 0\)), the natural log evaluates to a massive negative value, and the leading negative sign flips it into a huge loss penalty that drives your gradient descent step forward.

    Textbook Notes Study Guide

    The Generalist's Mathematical Blueprint: 1. The Link Function Correction: Linear regressions cannot perform classification directly due to a range space mismatch. We deploy non-linear link functions—the Sigmoid for binary targets and the Softmax for multi-class targets—to compress unbounded real inner products down to valid probability spaces. 2. The Transcendental Optimization Trap: Introducing non-linear link functions breaks our ability to use closed-form matrix inversions. We must use numerical optimization routines like First-Order Gradient Descent to solve the parameter weights iteratively. 3. The Cross-Entropy Selection Rule: For multi-class tasks, we swap out squared error metrics for Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss (\(-\sum y_k \ln h_k\)). This loss uses our one-hot target vectors as a clean selector mask to evaluate and optimize our model's predictive accuracy.

    This is the absolute peak of the course's grand duality theme. The reason the cross-entropy selection rule works—and the exact way it connects to everything we have built so far—comes down to a single mathematical rule: Every time you pick a deterministic loss function in the Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) world, you are secretly choosing a probability distribution in the Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) world.

    In Chapter 18, we proved that minimizing Squared Error Loss (\(L_2\)) is the mathematically exact equivalent of assuming your target data follows a continuous Gaussian Distribution.

    The cross-entropy selection rule is simply the multi-class categorical twin of that exact same trick. Let's derive it step-by-step from first principles to see exactly how it comes about.

    Step 1: Identify the Distribution of a One-Hot Target

    In a multi-class classification problem with \(M\) classes, our label \(\vec{y}\) is a discrete, one-hot encoded vector. If an instance belongs to class \(c\), then the \(c\)-th element of the vector is \(1\), and all other \(M - 1\) elements are strictly \(0\).

    The standard statistical distribution that governs a single trial of a multi-class discrete variable is the Categorical (or Multinoulli) Distribution.

    Let \(\vec{\pi} = [\pi_1, \pi_2, \dots, \pi_M]^T\) be a vector tracking the true, hidden probabilities of landing on each of the \(M\) classes (where \(\sum \pi_k = 1\)). The formal Probability Mass Function (PMF) for a single observed one-hot vector \(\vec{y}\) is written compactly as a product string:

    \[p(\vec{y} \mid \vec{\pi}) = \prod_{k=1}^{M} \pi_k^{y_k}\]

    Why this notation trick works: If our data point belongs to Class 2, then \(\vec{y} = [0, 1, 0]^T\). Plugs this directly into the product string: $\(p(\vec{y} \mid \vec{\pi}) = \pi_1^0 \cdot \pi_2^1 \cdot \pi_3^0 = 1 \cdot \pi_2 \cdot 1 = \pi_2\)$

    The exponents act as perfect binary on/off switches, automatically isolating the probability of the active class while ignoring the rest.

    Step 2: Inject the Softmax Hypothesis Architecture

    In our machine learning model, we don't know the true probability parameters \(\vec{\pi}\). Instead, we use our network's Softmax Output Layer (\(h_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{x})\)) to estimate them.

    The model declares that the predicted probability for the \(k\)-th class is:

    \[\pi_k \approx h_k = \left( h_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{x}) \right)_k\]

    Substituting our model's predictions directly into the Categorical PMF gives us the explicit Conditional Likelihood for a single data point:

    \[p_{\mathbf{\Theta}}(\vec{y} \mid \vec{x}) = \prod_{k=1}^{M} (h_k)^{y_k}\]

    Step 3: Run Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

    Now, let us scale this to evaluate an entire dataset of \(N\) independent, identically distributed (i.i.d.) sample rows. To find the optimal parameters \(\mathbf{\Theta}^*\), we invoke our standard MLE ledger, multiplying the individual likelihoods together:

    \[P(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}; \,\, \mathbf{\Theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \prod_{k=1}^{M} (h_{i,k})^{y_{i,k}}\]

    To break the product chain down into a manageable addition field, we take the natural logarithm of both sides:

    \[\ln P(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}; \,\, \mathbf{\Theta}) = \ln \left( \prod_{i=1}^{N} \prod_{k=1}^{M} (h_{i,k})^{y_{i,k}} \right)\]

    Using standard logarithmic properties (\(\ln(a \cdot b) = \ln a + \ln b\) and \(\ln a^b = b \ln a\)), the logarithm passes directly through the product operators, transforming them into summations and pulling the exponents down:

    \[\ln P(\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}; \,\, \mathbf{\Theta}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{k=1}^{M} y_{i,k} \ln(h_{i,k})\]

    Step 4: The Convergence onto Categorical Cross-Entropy

    To convert this probabilistic maximization target into a standard ERM minimization problem, we multiply the entire equation by the negative scaling factor \(-\frac{1}{N}\):

    \[\text{Cost}(\mathbf{\Theta}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( -\sum_{k=1}^{M} y_{i,k} \ln(h_{i,k}) \right)\]

    Look at the formula trapped inside the large parenthesis. It is the exact mathematical definition of Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss (\(L_{\text{CE}}\)) for an individual data instance:

    \[L_{\text{CE}}(\vec{y}, \,\, \vec{h}) = -\sum_{k=1}^{M} y_k \ln(h_k)\]

    The riddle is solved. Cross-entropy loss was not pulled out of thin air by software engineers. It is the inescapable mathematical byproduct of running Maximum Likelihood Estimation under the assumption that your target labels follow a discrete Categorical distribution.

    The Rosetta Stone of Aligned Frameworks

    To see how these concepts operate together in perfect symmetry, look at how the entire course path splits down the line between these two dual vocabularies:

    Target Variable Landscape The Probabilistic Guess (MLE View) The Loss Function Wrapper (ERM View) The Optimization Topology
    Continuous Real Continuum (\(y \in \mathbb{R}^1\)) Homoscedastic Gaussian Distribution assumption. Squared Error Loss (\(L_2\) Norm / OLS). Smooth, quadratic convex bowl; solvable via closed-form matrix inversion.
    Discrete Categorical Simplex (\(y \in \{1, \dots, M\}\)) Discrete Categorical Distribution assumption. Categorical Cross-Entropy Loss. Transcendental non-linear surface; requires iterative first-order Gradient Descent.

    When you choose cross-entropy, you are aligning your loss engine with the exact discrete physics of your categorical data space.
