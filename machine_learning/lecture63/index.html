    Chapter 66: Foundations of Generative Modeling

    66.1 From Discriminative to Generative: A Shift in Goals

    Throughout this course, the central object of study has been a fixed but unknown probability distribution \(p(\mathbf{x})\) defined over some space \(\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\). We are never given \(p(\mathbf{x})\) directly. What we are given is a dataset

    \[\mathcal{D} = \{\mathbf{x}^{(1)}, \mathbf{x}^{(2)}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}^{(n)}\}\]

    where each \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)} \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} p(\mathbf{x})\). Every method we have studied — maximum likelihood estimation, kernel density estimation, neural network classifiers, support vector machines — has been, at its mathematical core, an attempt to estimate \(p(\mathbf{x})\) or some functional of it from these \(n\) samples.

    Recall the two broad regimes we identified:

    Supervised (discriminative) learning estimates the conditional distribution \(p(y \mid \mathbf{x})\). We observe pairs \((\mathbf{x}^{(i)}, y^{(i)})\) and use them to construct an approximation \(p_\theta(y \mid \mathbf{x})\). A neural network trained with cross-entropy loss does this implicitly, without ever committing to a parametric family for the full joint \(p(\mathbf{x}, y)\).

    Unsupervised learning estimates the marginal \(p(\mathbf{x})\) — the distribution of the data itself, with no labels.

    Generative models live in the unsupervised regime, but they impose a strictly stronger requirement than mere distribution estimation:

    Definition 66.1 (Goal of Generative Modeling). Given \(\mathcal{D} \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} p(\mathbf{x})\), a generative model must: 1. Estimate \(p(\mathbf{x})\), and 2. Provide an efficient algorithm to draw new samples \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \sim p(\mathbf{x})\).

    The second requirement is what distinguishes generative models from the discriminative and density-estimation methods we have already studied. A kernel density estimator, for instance, gives us an estimate \(\hat{p}(\mathbf{x})\) that we can evaluate at any point, but drawing a fresh sample from \(\hat{p}\) requires additional work. A generative model, by definition, is constructed so that sampling is a first-class operation.

    66.2 Taxonomy: Explicit vs. Implicit Density Estimation

    The same explicit/implicit dichotomy we introduced for discriminative models applies here.

    Explicit generative models commit to a parametric family \(\{p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) : \theta \in \Theta\}\) and fit \(\theta\) by some form of divergence minimization (most commonly maximum likelihood, which minimizes the KL divergence from the empirical distribution to the model). Once \(\theta\) is fit, sampling requires inverting or simulating the assumed family.

    Example. A Gaussian mixture model (GMM) is an explicit generative model. We assume

    \[p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)\]

    with \(\theta = \{\pi_k, \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k\}_{k=1}^K\). Maximum likelihood via the EM algorithm gives \(\hat\theta\). Sampling from \(p_{\hat\theta}\) is easy: draw a component \(k \sim \text{Categorical}(\hat\pi_1, \ldots, \hat\pi_K)\), then draw \(\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}}_k, \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}_k)\).

    Implicit generative models never write down a tractable closed form for \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\). Instead, they define a procedure to generate samples, and the distribution that procedure induces on \(\mathcal{X}\) is \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) — even if \(p_\theta\) has no closed-form expression. We will see that the most powerful modern architectures — GANs, VAEs, and autoregressive models — are all, in different ways, implicit or semi-implicit.

    66.3 Conditional vs. Unconditional Generation

    Just as supervised learning can be seen as conditional distribution estimation, generative models can operate conditionally or unconditionally.

    Unconditional generation models and samples from the marginal \(p(\mathbf{x})\). The model receives no additional information specifying what kind of sample to produce. The website thispersonisnotexist.com is a canonical example: you send a random noise vector \(\mathbf{z}\) and receive a photorealistic human face sampled from \(p(\mathbf{x})\), the marginal distribution of human face images.

    Conditional generation models and samples from a conditional distribution \(p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y})\), where \(\mathbf{y}\) is some conditioning variable (a text prompt, a class label, a partially observed image, etc.). Mathematically:

    \[\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \sim p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{y}_0)\]

    where \(\mathbf{y}_0\) is the specific conditioning value provided at inference time.

    Example: Text-to-image generation. Here \(\mathbf{y}\) is a text embedding (a vector representation of a caption such as "a cat sitting on a red sofa") and \(\mathbf{x}\) is a high-dimensional image vector. The model learns \(p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y})\) from millions of (caption, image) pairs.

    Example: Large Language Models. An LLM is a conditional generative model over token sequences. Given a prompt \(\mathbf{y} = (y_1, \ldots, y_t)\), it models

    \[p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y}) = p(x_{t+1}, x_{t+2}, \ldots \mid y_1, \ldots, y_t)\]

    and generates by sequentially sampling one token at a time:

    \[x_{t+1} \sim p(x_{t+1} \mid y_1, \ldots, y_t), \quad x_{t+2} \sim p(x_{t+2} \mid y_1, \ldots, y_t, x_{t+1}), \quad \ldots\]

    This is the autoregressive sampling paradigm, and we will return to it in Section 66.8.

    The mathematical tools for unconditional generation are foundational and apply, with minor modifications, to the conditional case as well. We therefore develop the unconditional theory first.

    66.4 The Sampling Problem: A Precise Formulation

    Before we can build generative models, we need to be precise about what it means to "sample from a distribution."

    Recall from Chapter 1: a probability space is a triple \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\). A random variable \(X : \Omega \to \mathcal{X}\) is a measurable function from the sample space \(\Omega\) to the data space \(\mathcal{X}\). The distribution \(p(\mathbf{x})\) is the pushforward of \(\mathbb{P}\) through \(X\): for any measurable set \(A \subseteq \mathcal{X}\),

    \[p(A) = \mathbb{P}(X^{-1}(A)) = \mathbb{P}(\{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in A\})\]

    Our data \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)} \in \mathcal{X}\) are elements of the range of \(X\). They came from conducting the underlying random experiment \(n\) times. To generate new data, we need to conduct that random experiment again — but we do not have access to \(\Omega\), to \(\mathbb{P}\), or to the experiment itself. All we have is \(\mathcal{D}\), the observed outputs \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)} \in \mathcal{X}\).

    The sampling problem: Given \(\mathcal{D} = \{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^n\) drawn i.i.d. from an unknown \(p(\mathbf{x})\), produce new points \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \in \mathcal{X}\) such that \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \sim p(\mathbf{x})\) — that is, such that the law of \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\) is \(p\).

    The phrase "such that the law of \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\) is \(p\)" is crucial. It is not sufficient that \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \in \mathcal{D}\); that would make the generative model a lookup table. We want the model to generalize: to produce points that \(p\) assigns high probability to, including points never seen in \(\mathcal{D}\).

    The correct criterion is distributional: the empirical distribution of infinitely many samples from our model should converge to \(p(\mathbf{x})\). In other words, if \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(1)}, \tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(2)}, \ldots\) are generated by our model, then for any measurable \(A\),

    \[\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=1}^m \mathbf{1}[\tilde{\mathbf{x}}^{(j)} \in A] \xrightarrow{m \to \infty} p(A)\]

    This rules out trivial solutions (like memorizing \(\mathcal{D}\)) and sets the correct target for learning.

    66.5 The Universal Starting Point: Uniform Sampling

    Every generative model, regardless of its architecture or family, rests on a single foundational assumption:

    Assumption 66.1. We know how to sample from the uniform distribution \(\text{Uniform}[0,1]\).

    This assumption is taken as given. In practice, it is satisfied by pseudo-random number generators (PRNGs) — deterministic algorithms that produce sequences of numbers statistically indistinguishable from \(\text{Uniform}[0,1]\) samples. The famous Mersenne Twister, linear congruential generators, and modern cryptographic PRNGs all serve this purpose. There is a deep irony here: we produce "randomness" using purely deterministic computation.

    Everything else — sampling from a Gaussian, a Gamma, a Dirichlet, or a learned neural network distribution — reduces to: 1. Draw \(Z \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\) (or a vector \(\mathbf{Z} \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]^k\)), and 2. Apply a deterministic transformation \(G\) to \(\mathbf{Z}\) so that \(G(\mathbf{Z})\) has the target distribution.

    The question is: what should \(G\) be?

    66.6 The Fundamental Theorem of Generative Modeling

    We now state and prove the core result that underlies all generative modeling.

    66.6.1 The Push-Forward Principle

    Theorem 66.1 (Push-Forward Principle). Let \(\mathbf{Z} \sim p_Z(\mathbf{z})\) be a random variable on \(\mathcal{Z}\), and let \(G_\theta : \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}\) be a (measurable) deterministic function parameterized by \(\theta\). Define \(\tilde{\mathbf{X}} = G_\theta(\mathbf{Z})\). Then \(\tilde{\mathbf{X}}\) is a random variable on \(\mathcal{X}\), and its distribution \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) — the push-forward of \(p_Z\) through \(G_\theta\) — is generally different from \(p_Z\).

    Proof sketch. \(\tilde{X}\) is a random variable because it is the composition of two measurable functions: \(\omega \mapsto Z(\omega)\) and \(G_\theta\). The composite \(\omega \mapsto G_\theta(Z(\omega))\) is measurable, hence a random variable. For any measurable \(A \subseteq \mathcal{X}\),

    \[p_\theta(A) = \mathbb{P}(\tilde{X} \in A) = \mathbb{P}(Z \in G_\theta^{-1}(A)) = p_Z(G_\theta^{-1}(A))\]

    which in general differs from \(p_Z(A)\) unless \(G_\theta\) is the identity. \(\blacksquare\)

    Consequence. If we can find \(\theta^*\) such that the push-forward distribution \(p_{\theta^*}\) matches the target \(p(\mathbf{x})\), then the following procedure samples from \(p(\mathbf{x})\):

    Generative Sampling Procedure: 1. Draw \(\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z(\mathbf{z})\) (our known distribution, e.g. \(\text{Uniform}[0,1]^k\) or \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\)). 2. Compute \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z})\). 3. Output \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\).

    This is the entire intellectual foundation of generative modeling.

    66.6.2 Finding \(\theta^*\): Divergence Minimization

    How do we find \(\theta^*\)? We minimize a divergence (or dissimilarity measure) between \(p_\theta\) and \(p\):

    \[\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta \, D(p \,\|\, p_\theta)\]

    where \(D(\cdot \,\|\, \cdot)\) is some measure of discrepancy between distributions. Different choices of \(D\) give different generative model families:

    Choice of \(D\) Model Family
    KL divergence \(D_{\text{KL}}(p \| p_\theta)\) Maximum Likelihood / VAE (ELBO)
    Jensen-Shannon divergence GAN (original formulation)
    Wasserstein distance Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)
    \(f\)-divergences \(f\)-GAN family
    Score matching Score-based / Diffusion models

    The design choices reduce to three fundamental questions:

    1. What is \(G_\theta\)? (Architecture of the generator)
    2. What is \(D\)? (Which divergence to minimize)
    3. How do we solve \(\min_\theta D(p \| p_\theta)\)? (Optimization, given that we cannot evaluate \(p\))

    The entire literature on generative models is, in a precise sense, a catalogue of different answers to these three questions.

    66.7 The Inverse CDF Method: A First Concrete Example

    To make the Push-Forward Principle concrete before we introduce neural networks, we examine the classical Inverse CDF (Probability Integral Transform) method. It is the simplest instance of the framework and illuminates why the general approach works.

    66.7.1 The Probability Integral Transform

    Theorem 66.2 (Probability Integral Transform). Let \(X\) be a continuous random variable with CDF \(F_X(x) = \mathbb{P}(X \leq x)\). Define \(U = F_X(X)\). Then \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\).

    Proof. For \(u \in [0,1]\):

    \[\mathbb{P}(U \leq u) = \mathbb{P}(F_X(X) \leq u) = \mathbb{P}(X \leq F_X^{-1}(u)) = F_X(F_X^{-1}(u)) = u\]

    where the third equality uses the fact that \(F_X\) is non-decreasing and the last equality uses the definition of the inverse. Since \(\mathbb{P}(U \leq u) = u\) for all \(u \in [0,1]\), we have \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\). \(\blacksquare\)

    66.7.2 The Inverse Transform Method (Sampling)

    Corollary 66.1 (Inverse Transform Sampling). Let \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\) and define \(X = F^{-1}(U)\), where \(F\) is the CDF of a target distribution. Then \(X\) has CDF \(F\).

    Proof.

    \[\mathbb{P}(X \leq x) = \mathbb{P}(F^{-1}(U) \leq x) = \mathbb{P}(U \leq F(x)) = F(x)\]

    where the last equality uses \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\). \(\blacksquare\)

    This is exactly the Push-Forward Principle with \(\mathcal{Z} = [0,1]\), \(p_Z = \text{Uniform}[0,1]\), and \(G_\theta = F^{-1}\) (the quantile function of the target).

    Worked Example 66.1: Sampling from an Exponential Distribution.

    Let the target be \(X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)\), with PDF \(p(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \geq 0\).

    Step 1: Compute the CDF. $\(F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\)$

    Step 2: Invert. $\(u = 1 - e^{-\lambda x} \implies e^{-\lambda x} = 1 - u \implies x = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1-u)\)$

    So \(G = F^{-1}(u) = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1-u)\).

    Step 3: Sample. Draw \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\) and compute \(X = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1-U)\).

    Since \(1-U \stackrel{d}{=} U\) for \(U \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\), in practice we use \(X = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(U)\) for simplicity.

    Verification via Change of Variables. Let \(X = G(U) = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(U)\). The PDF of \(X\) is: $\(p_X(x) = p_U(G^{-1}(x)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dx}G^{-1}(x)\right|\)$ where \(G^{-1}(x) = e^{-\lambda x}\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}e^{-\lambda x} = -\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\).

    \[p_X(x) = 1 \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda x} = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \checkmark\]

    66.7.3 Limitations and the Motivation for Learned \(G_\theta\)

    The inverse CDF method requires that we know \(F\) (and therefore \(p\)) in closed form, and that \(F^{-1}\) is computable. Neither condition holds for high-dimensional data distributions like the distribution of natural images, text, or audio.

    For a \(512 \times 512\) color image, \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{786432}\). We have no formula for \(p(\mathbf{x})\). What we have is \(\mathcal{D}\). This is the gap that modern generative models bridge: they learn the function \(G_\theta\) from data, using a parametric family (neural networks) flexible enough to approximate any measurable \(G\).

    66.8 Generalization of the Inverse CDF: Learned Generators

    The Inverse CDF method identified the right structure: - Start from a simple distribution \(p_Z\) (Uniform). - Apply a deterministic function \(G\) (the quantile function). - The result has the target distribution.

    The leap to modern generative models is to replace the analytically computed \(G = F^{-1}\) with a learned function \(G_\theta\), parameterized by a deep neural network, trained to minimize \(D(p \| p_\theta)\).

    66.8.1 Why Any Measurable \(G_\theta\) Works in Principle

    Theorem 66.3 (Universality). For any two distributions \(p_Z\) on \(\mathcal{Z}\) and \(p\) on \(\mathcal{X}\), there exists a measurable function \(G^* : \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}\) such that if \(\mathbf{Z} \sim p_Z\), then \(G^*(\mathbf{Z}) \sim p\).

    This is guaranteed by the universal property of measurable functions and the Borel isomorphism theorem for Polish spaces. It tells us that the space of measurable functions is rich enough — in principle, \(G^*\) exists. The practical question is how to find it from data.

    Remark. \(G_\theta\) is typically chosen to be a neural network, because neural networks are universal approximators: they can approximate any continuous function on a compact domain to arbitrary precision (Hornik, 1991). Combined with Theorem 66.3, this means neural networks are expressive enough to represent any generator \(G^*\).

    66.8.2 The Change-of-Variables Formula for Neural Generators

    When \(G_\theta : \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d\) is differentiable and bijective (with \(k = d\)), the density of \(\tilde{\mathbf{X}} = G_\theta(\mathbf{Z})\) is given exactly by the change-of-variables formula:

    \[p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(G_\theta^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \cdot \left|\det J_{G_\theta^{-1}}(\mathbf{x})\right|\]

    where \(J_{G_\theta^{-1}}\) is the Jacobian matrix of \(G_\theta^{-1}\). This formula is the engine of the Normalizing Flows family of generative models (which we will not cover in this introductory chapter, but which appear in the full generative modeling course).

    When \(G_\theta\) is not invertible or when \(k < d\) (the generator maps from a lower-dimensional latent space to a higher-dimensional data space), the change-of-variables formula does not apply directly, and we need alternative ways to define or approximate the divergence \(D(p \| p_\theta)\). This is precisely the setting where GANs and VAEs come in.

    66.9 Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

    We now introduce the first of the three major generative model families: Generative Adversarial Networks, proposed by Goodfellow et al. (2014).

    66.9.1 Setup and Motivation

    Let \(\mathbf{Z} \sim p_Z(\mathbf{z})\), typically \(\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) (a \(k\)-dimensional standard normal, called the latent space). Let \(G_\theta : \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^d\) be the generator, a neural network. The generator defines an implicit distribution \(p_\theta\) on \(\mathbb{R}^d\) via the push-forward.

    We want to find \(\theta\) such that \(p_\theta \approx p\). The challenge: we cannot compute \(p_\theta\) in closed form when \(G_\theta\) is a deep network with \(k < d\), so we cannot directly minimize \(D_{\text{KL}}(p \| p_\theta)\) either.

    The GAN Idea. Instead of estimating the density \(p_\theta\) directly, train a second neural network — the discriminator \(D_\phi : \mathbb{R}^d \to [0,1]\) — to distinguish samples from \(p_\theta\) (fake) from samples from \(p\) (real). Use the discriminator's signal to train the generator \(G_\theta\).

    66.9.2 The GAN Objective: Deriving the Value Function

    We will derive the GAN objective from first principles, starting from the Jensen-Shannon divergence.

    Definition 66.2 (Jensen-Shannon Divergence). For distributions \(p\) and \(q\):

    \[D_{\text{JS}}(p \| q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(p \,\Big\|\, \frac{p+q}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2} D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(q \,\Big\|\, \frac{p+q}{2}\right)\]

    \(D_{\text{JS}}\) is symmetric, bounded in \([0, \ln 2]\), and equals \(0\) if and only if \(p = q\).

    Claim. The GAN minimax game is equivalent to minimizing \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta)\).

    To see this, consider the following minimax optimization:

    \[\min_\theta \max_\phi \, V(G_\theta, D_\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[\ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x})] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))]\]

    Step 1: Optimal discriminator for fixed \(G_\theta\).

    Fix \(G_\theta\) and maximize \(V\) over \(D_\phi\). The discriminator sees each point \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) and must output a value. The objective decomposes point-wise:

    \[\max_{D_\phi} \int_{\mathcal{X}} \left[ p(\mathbf{x}) \ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \ln(1 - D_\phi(\mathbf{x})) \right] d\mathbf{x}\]

    For a fixed \(\mathbf{x}\), maximizing \(a \ln y + b \ln(1-y)\) over \(y \in [0,1]\) gives:

    \[\frac{d}{dy}[a \ln y + b \ln(1-y)] = \frac{a}{y} - \frac{b}{1-y} = 0 \implies y^* = \frac{a}{a+b}\]

    Therefore, the optimal discriminator is:

    \[\boxed{D_{\phi^*}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}}\]

    Interpretation. At the optimal discriminator, \(D_{\phi^*}(\mathbf{x})\) is exactly the posterior probability that \(\mathbf{x}\) came from \(p\) (real) rather than \(p_\theta\) (fake). If \(p = p_\theta\), then \(D_{\phi^*}(\mathbf{x}) = 1/2\) everywhere — the discriminator cannot tell real from fake.

    Step 2: Substituting \(D_{\phi^*}\) into \(V\).

    \[V(G_\theta, D_{\phi^*}) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}\!\left[\ln \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}\right] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta}\!\left[\ln \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}\right]\]

    Adding and subtracting \(\ln 2\) inside each expectation:

    \[= \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}\!\left[\ln \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{\frac{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{2}} - \ln 2\right] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_\theta}\!\left[\ln \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{\frac{p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x})}{2}} - \ln 2\right]\]
    \[= D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(p \,\Big\|\, \frac{p + p_\theta}{2}\right) + D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(p_\theta \,\Big\|\, \frac{p + p_\theta}{2}\right) - 2\ln 2\]
    \[= 2 \cdot D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta) - 2\ln 2\]

    Therefore:

    \[\min_\theta \max_\phi V(G_\theta, D_\phi) = \min_\theta \left[2 \cdot D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta) - 2\ln 2\right]\]

    Minimizing over \(\theta\) minimizes \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta)\), which achieves its minimum value of \(0\) if and only if \(p_\theta = p\). At that point, \(V^* = -2\ln 2\).

    Summary: The GAN framework in three lines.

    \[\max_\phi \, V = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p}[\ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x})] + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))] \quad \text{(Discriminator step)}\]
    \[\min_\theta \, V = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))] \quad \text{(Generator step)}\]
    \[\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z}), \quad \mathbf{z} \sim p_Z \quad \text{(Sampling)}\]

    66.9.3 Worked Example: GAN on a 1D Gaussian

    To build intuition without high-dimensional complexity, consider \(p = \mathcal{N}(5, 1)\) (target) and \(p_Z = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\) (latent). Let \(G_\theta(z) = \theta_1 z + \theta_2\) (affine generator). Then:

    \[p_\theta = \mathcal{N}(\theta_2, \theta_1^2)\]

    The true distribution is \(\mathcal{N}(5, 1)\), so we need \(\theta_1^* = 1\) (scale) and \(\theta_2^* = 5\) (shift).

    The GAN would train a discriminator \(D_\phi : \mathbb{R} \to [0,1]\) to distinguish samples from \(\mathcal{N}(5,1)\) from samples of \(G_\theta(z) = \theta_1 z + \theta_2\) for \(z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). The generator \(G_\theta\) is trained to fool \(D_\phi\) — i.e., to make \(D_\phi(G_\theta(z)) \approx 1\) for all \(z\).

    At convergence, \(D_\phi(x) = 1/2\) for all \(x\), and \(\tilde{x} = G_{\theta^*}(z) = z + 5 \sim \mathcal{N}(5, 1) = p\). The generator has learned to sample from the target by learning the correct affine map from the known latent to the unknown target.

    66.9.4 Training Procedure and Practical Considerations

    In practice, we do not have access to \(p\) or \(p_\theta\) in closed form — only to finite samples. The GAN training loop approximates the expectations with mini-batch averages:

    Algorithm 66.1: GAN Training (Mini-batch SGD)

    For each training iteration: 1. Sample a mini-batch of real data: \(\{\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim p\) 2. Sample a mini-batch of latent vectors: \(\{\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim p_Z\) 3. Compute discriminator loss: $\(\hat{L}_D = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \left[\ln D_\phi(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}) + \ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)})))\right]\)$ 4. Update \(\phi\) by gradient descent: \(\phi \leftarrow \phi - \eta \nabla_\phi \hat{L}_D\) 5. Sample fresh \(\{\mathbf{z}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim p_Z\) 6. Compute generator loss (equivalently, use the non-saturating variant): $\(\hat{L}_G = -\frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ln D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}^{(i)}))\)$ 7. Update \(\theta\) by gradient descent: \(\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \hat{L}_G\)

    Remark on the non-saturating loss. The original generator loss \(\mathbb{E}[\ln(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})))]\) saturates early in training: when \(D_\phi\) confidently rejects \(G_\theta\)'s outputs, \(D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z})) \approx 0\), and \(\ln(1-0) = 0\) produces vanishingly small gradients. The fix is to maximize \(\mathbb{E}[\ln D_\phi(G_\theta(\mathbf{z}))]\) instead (equivalently, minimize the negative), which provides stronger gradients even when the discriminator is winning. This is the most common formulation in practice.

    66.10 Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

    The second major family uses a different answer to our three questions: explicit density estimation via a lower bound, with a learned encoder-decoder architecture.

    66.10.1 The Problem: Intractable Marginals

    We want to model \(p(\mathbf{x})\) using a latent variable \(\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^k\). The generative story is:

    \[\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}) \quad \text{(prior on latent)}$$ $$\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z} \sim p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) \quad \text{(decoder / likelihood)}\]

    The marginal likelihood is:

    \[p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) \, p(\mathbf{z}) \, d\mathbf{z}\]

    This integral is intractable for deep neural network decoders \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})\), because the latent space is high-dimensional and the integrand is not in closed form.

    Direct maximum likelihood would require:

    \[\max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n \ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}) = \max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n \ln \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x}^{(i)} \mid \mathbf{z}) \, p(\mathbf{z}) \, d\mathbf{z}\]

    which is intractable. We need a surrogate.

    66.10.2 The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)

    Introduce an approximate posterior \(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})\) — a neural network that maps data \(\mathbf{x}\) to a distribution over latent codes \(\mathbf{z}\). This is the encoder.

    For any \(q_\phi\), by Jensen's inequality:

    \[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \ln \int p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z}) \, d\mathbf{z}\]
    \[= \ln \int q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \cdot \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})} \, d\mathbf{z}\]
    \[= \ln \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\!\left[\frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\right]\]
    \[\geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\!\left[\ln \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\right] \quad \text{(Jensen, since }\ln\text{ is concave)}\]

    This lower bound is the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

    \[\mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; \mathbf{x}) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \| p(\mathbf{z}))\]

    Derivation of the KL form. Expand the expectation:

    \[\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\ln \frac{p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z}) p(\mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\right] = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}\!\left[\ln \frac{p(\mathbf{z})}{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}\right]\]
    \[= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})] - D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \| p(\mathbf{z}))\]

    Interpretation of the two terms:

    The reconstruction term \(\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})]\) measures how well the decoder \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})\) can reconstruct \(\mathbf{x}\) from latent codes sampled from the encoder. It rewards the encoder-decoder pair for faithfully representing the data.

    The regularization term \(-D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \| p(\mathbf{z}))\) penalizes the encoder's posterior \(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})\) for deviating from the prior \(p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\). This keeps the latent space well-organized and ensures that we can sample \(\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z})\) and decode it to a plausible \(\mathbf{x}\).

    The exact gap. We can show:

    \[\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{L}(\theta, \phi; \mathbf{x}) + D_{\text{KL}}(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) \| p_\theta(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}))\]

    Since \(D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0\), we confirm \(\mathcal{L} \leq \ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\), with equality when \(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) = p_\theta(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})\) (the approximate posterior equals the true posterior).

    66.10.3 The Reparameterization Trick

    To train both \(\theta\) and \(\phi\) jointly by gradient descent, we need \(\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}\). The difficulty: the expectation \(\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}[\cdot]\) depends on \(\phi\) (the distribution we sample from), so we cannot simply push the gradient through the expectation.

    Solution: The Reparameterization Trick. Choose \(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}), \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2_\phi(\mathbf{x})))\), where \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi\) and \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi\) are outputs of the encoder neural network. Instead of sampling \(\mathbf{z} \sim q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})\) directly, write:

    \[\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\]

    Now \(\mathbf{z}\) is a deterministic function of \(\phi\), \(\mathbf{x}\), and \(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\) (which does not depend on \(\phi\)). Therefore:

    \[\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})}[f(\mathbf{z})] = \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})}[f(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) + \boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi(\mathbf{x}) \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon})]\]

    and we can compute \(\nabla_\phi\) by differentiating through \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_\phi\) and \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}_\phi\).

    66.10.4 Closed-Form KL for Gaussian Prior and Posterior

    When both prior \(p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\) and posterior \(q_\phi(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2))\) are Gaussian, the KL term has a closed form:

    \[D_{\text{KL}}(\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2)) \| \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^k \left(\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - 1 - \ln \sigma_j^2\right)\]

    Derivation (for scalar case, \(k=1\)). Let \(q = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) and \(p = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\).

    \[D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \int q(z) \ln \frac{q(z)}{p(z)} \, dz = \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[\ln q(z) - \ln p(z)\right]\]
    \[= \mathbb{E}_q\!\left[-\frac{(z-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} - \frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi\sigma^2) + \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi)\right]\]
    \[= -\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\ln\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mathbb{E}_q[z^2]}{2}\]

    Since \(\mathbb{E}_q[z^2] = \text{Var}_q(z) + (\mathbb{E}_q[z])^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2\):

    \[D_{\text{KL}}(q \| p) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mu^2 + \sigma^2 - 1 - \ln\sigma^2\right) \checkmark\]

    The vector case follows by independence across dimensions.

    66.10.5 VAE Sampling at Test Time

    Once training is complete, to generate a new sample \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\):

    1. Draw \(\mathbf{z} \sim p(\mathbf{z}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})\) (no encoder needed at test time).
    2. Decode: \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \sim p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{z})\).

    This is exactly the Push-Forward framework: a noise vector \(\mathbf{z}\) from a known distribution is passed through a learned deterministic function (the decoder) to produce a sample from (approximately) \(p(\mathbf{x})\).

    66.11 Autoregressive Models

    The third family approaches the problem from a completely different direction: explicit density estimation using the chain rule of probability.

    66.11.1 The Chain Rule Decomposition

    Any joint distribution over \(d\) variables can be factored as:

    \[p(\mathbf{x}) = p(x_1) \cdot p(x_2 \mid x_1) \cdot p(x_3 \mid x_1, x_2) \cdots p(x_d \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{d-1}) = \prod_{j=1}^d p(x_j \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1})\]

    This is not an approximation — it is an exact identity from the definition of conditional probability. It holds for any ordering of the variables and any distribution \(p\).

    An autoregressive model parameterizes each conditional:

    \[p_\theta(x_j \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1}) = f_\theta^{(j)}(x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1})\]

    where \(f_\theta^{(j)}\) is a neural network that takes the previous \(j-1\) variables as input and outputs a distribution over \(x_j\).

    66.11.2 Autoregressive Sampling

    Sampling from an autoregressive model is sequential:

    1. Sample \(x_1 \sim p_\theta(x_1)\) (an unconditional distribution).
    2. Sample \(x_2 \sim p_\theta(x_2 \mid x_1)\).
    3. Sample \(x_j \sim p_\theta(x_j \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1})\) for \(j = 3, \ldots, d\).
    4. Return \(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d)\).

    Each step uses the Push-Forward principle: given \(u \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]\), apply the inverse CDF of \(p_\theta(x_j \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{j-1})\) to get \(x_j\).

    66.11.3 Language Models as Autoregressive Samplers

    In the language modeling setting, \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T)\) is a sequence of tokens (subword units) from a vocabulary \(\mathcal{V}\) with \(|\mathcal{V}| \approx 50{,}000\). The model factorizes:

    \[p_\theta(x_1, \ldots, x_T) = \prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})\]

    Each conditional \(p_\theta(x_t \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{t-1})\) is a categorical distribution over \(|\mathcal{V}|\) tokens, output by a Transformer network.

    Training objective. Maximize the log-likelihood over the training corpus:

    \[\max_\theta \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \sum_{t=1}^{T_i} \ln p_\theta(x_t^{(i)} \mid x_1^{(i)}, \ldots, x_{t-1}^{(i)})\]

    This is equivalent to minimizing the cross-entropy between the empirical distribution of the corpus and \(p_\theta\), which in turn minimizes \(D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_\theta)\).

    Conditional generation. A prompted LLM computes \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y})\) where \(\mathbf{y}\) is the prompt. The factorization continues from position \(T_y + 1\):

    \[p_\theta(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{y}) = \prod_{t=T_y+1}^{T} p_\theta(x_t \mid y_1, \ldots, y_{T_y}, x_{T_y+1}, \ldots, x_{t-1})\]

    Each call to the model is still a categorical distribution over \(|\mathcal{V}|\) tokens, and sampling is just drawing from that categorical.

    66.11.4 Example: Next-Token Prediction

    Suppose \(\mathcal{V} = \{A, B, C\}\) and we observe the corpus \(\mathcal{D} = \{\text{"ABACABA"}\}\) (treated as 7 tokens: \(A, B, A, C, A, B, A\)).

    A perfect autoregressive model would learn:

    Context Next token distribution
    (none) \(p(x_1 = A) = 1\)
    \(A\) \(p(B \mid A) = 2/3, \; p(C \mid A) = 1/3\)
    \(AB\) \(p(A \mid AB) = 1\)
    \(ABA\) \(p(C \mid ABA) = 1\)
    \(ABAC\) \(p(A \mid ABAC) = 1\)
    \(ABACA\) \(p(B \mid ABACA) = 1\)
    \(ABACAB\) \(p(A \mid ABACAB) = 1\)

    Sampling from this model: Draw \(x_1 = A\). Draw \(x_2 \sim p(\cdot \mid A) = \text{Categorical}(B: 2/3, C: 1/3)\). If \(x_2 = B\): draw \(x_3 = A\). Etc. This produces sequences that follow the statistical patterns of the training corpus, but generalizes beyond it (e.g., could generate "ABACA" which was not explicitly a training sequence).

    66.12 Evaluating Generative Models

    A critical subtlety: unlike discriminative models, we cannot evaluate a generative model by prediction accuracy on a held-out test set, because there are no labels. How do we measure whether \(p_\theta \approx p\)?

    66.12.1 Test Set Log-Likelihood

    For explicit models (autoregressive, VAE), we can evaluate the held-out log-likelihood:

    \[\text{NLL} = -\frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}|} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{D}_{\text{test}}} \ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\]

    This is the gold standard when tractable. A lower NLL indicates \(p_\theta\) assigns higher probability to real data — it has learned the distribution better. Note that the NLL minimization target is exactly \(D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_\theta)\) up to a constant (the entropy of \(p_{\text{data}}\)):

    \[D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_\theta) = H(p_{\text{data}}) + \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x} \sim p_{\text{data}}}[-\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})] = \text{const} + \text{NLL}\]

    For implicit models like GANs, there is no tractable \(\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\), so NLL is unavailable.

    66.12.2 Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)

    FID is the most common quantitative metric for image generative models. The idea: embed both real and generated images into a feature space using a pretrained Inception network, and measure the distance between the two distributions of embeddings.

    Formally, let \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_r, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r\) be the mean and covariance of real image features and \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_g, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g\) for generated features. The FID is the Fréchet (Wasserstein-2) distance between two Gaussians fit to these embeddings:

    \[\text{FID} = \|\boldsymbol{\mu}_r - \boldsymbol{\mu}_g\|^2 + \text{Tr}\!\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g - 2(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_g)^{1/2}\right)\]

    FID equals \(0\) when \(p_\theta = p\) (in the feature space). Lower is better. It can be computed for any model from which samples can be drawn, including GANs.

    Caveat. FID measures distributional similarity in a particular feature space (Inception features). A model could have low FID while still producing artifacts invisible to that feature extractor. It is a practical proxy, not a perfect measure of \(D(p \| p_\theta)\).

    66.12.3 Overfitting in Generative Models

    Recall the overfitting problem in discriminative models: a model that memorizes the training set achieves zero training loss but fails on new data. The analog in generative models is a model that simply stores \(\mathcal{D}\) and returns a random \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)}\) when asked to sample.

    Such a model would have very low (or zero) NLL on training data, but: - Would never generalize to points not in \(\mathcal{D}\). - Would assign zero probability to any \(\mathbf{x} \notin \mathcal{D}\). - In a continuous space, every sample from a good \(p_\theta\) should have \(\mathbb{P}(\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^{(i)}) = 0\) for any specific \(\mathbf{x}^{(i)} \in \mathcal{D}\) (since the probability of a single point under a continuous distribution is zero).

    The correct behavior is distributional: the model should put high density in regions where \(p(\mathbf{x})\) is high, not just on the specific samples it has seen. A held-out test set helps detect this, as a memorizing model will assign low likelihood to test points.

    66.13 Connecting the Three Families: A Unified View

    All three families — GANs, VAEs, and autoregressive models — instantiate the same underlying framework:

    \[\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta D(p \| p_\theta), \quad \tilde{\mathbf{x}} = G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{z}), \quad \mathbf{z} \sim p_Z\]

    They differ in the choices of \(G_\theta\), \(D\), and how the optimization is carried out:

    GAN VAE Autoregressive
    \(p_Z\) \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) \(\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_k)\) \(\text{Uniform}[0,1]^d\) (via inverse CDF at each step)
    \(G_\theta\) Deep network (implicit) Decoder network Sequential conditionals
    \(D\) Jensen-Shannon (approximated adversarially) KL (bounded via ELBO) KL (exact, via log-likelihood)
    Density \(p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) tractable? No No (ELBO is a bound) Yes
    Sampling One forward pass One forward pass \(d\) sequential forward passes
    Training stability Challenging (minimax) Stable (ELBO gradient) Stable (cross-entropy)

    This table is not just a summary — it reflects deep mathematical trade-offs. Explicit density estimation (autoregressive) gives tractable likelihoods but requires sequential sampling, which is slow for long sequences. Implicit estimation (GAN) enables fast one-shot sampling but provides no likelihood and is notoriously difficult to train due to the minimax objective. VAEs occupy a middle ground: they provide a tractable lower bound on the likelihood and fast sampling, but the bound is loose when the approximate posterior \(q_\phi\) is far from the true posterior.

    66.14 Summary

    This chapter established the mathematical foundations of generative modeling. The key ideas are:

    The goal of generative modeling is strictly stronger than density estimation: we must not only approximate \(p(\mathbf{x})\), but also be able to sample from it efficiently.

    The fundamental principle is the Push-Forward: learn a function \(G_{\theta^*} : \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}\) such that \(G_{\theta^*}(\mathbf{Z}) \sim p(\mathbf{x})\) for \(\mathbf{Z} \sim p_Z\), where \(p_Z\) is a known, easy-to-sample distribution. Finding \(\theta^*\) is cast as divergence minimization: \(\theta^* = \arg\min_\theta D(p \| p_\theta)\).

    The Inverse CDF method is the simplest concrete instance: \(G = F^{-1}\) (the quantile function). It works for known 1D distributions but fails for unknown high-dimensional distributions, motivating learned generators.

    GANs use an adversarial discriminator to approximate the Jensen-Shannon divergence without needing to compute \(p_\theta\) in closed form. The minimax game yields \(D_{\phi^*}(\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x})/(p(\mathbf{x}) + p_\theta(\mathbf{x}))\), and optimizing the generator is equivalent to minimizing \(D_{\text{JS}}(p \| p_\theta)\).

    VAEs use a latent variable model with an approximate posterior (encoder) and a decoder, trained by maximizing the ELBO — a tractable lower bound on \(\ln p_\theta(\mathbf{x})\) derived via Jensen's inequality. The reparameterization trick enables gradient flow through the sampling operation.

    Autoregressive models exploit the exact chain rule factorization \(p(\mathbf{x}) = \prod_j p(x_j \mid x_{< j})\), modeling each conditional with a neural network and sampling sequentially. Language models are the preeminent example.

    All three families answer the same three questions differently: what is \(G_\theta\), what is \(D\), and how is the optimization solved. The field of deep generative modeling is, in essence, the study of increasingly sophisticated answers to these three questions.

    End of Chapter 66. The following chapter examines training instabilities in GANs and the Wasserstein GAN formulation, which replaces the Jensen-Shannon divergence with the Wasserstein-1 distance to address the vanishing gradient problem.
