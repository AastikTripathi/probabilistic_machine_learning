    Mathematical Foundations of Machine Learning

    Chapter 44: Bootstrap Aggregation (Bagging), Statistical Covariance Overlapping, and the Foundations of Additive Gated Boosting

    44.1 The Philosophy of Committee-Based Estimation and Weak Learners

    In Chapter 43, we explored how non-parametric decision trees recursively partition high-dimensional space into regular hyper-rectangles. We proved that while unregularized trees can reduce structural bias to absolute zero, they pay a severe price in the form of extreme variance—effectively memorizing the training coordinates as a rigid lookup table.

    To systematically address this variance crisis without re-introducing high bias, we transition to Ensemble Methods. Ensemble theory frames machine learning not as a hunt for a single master hypothesis, but as a collaborative decision-making process driven by a committee of independent models.

    When constructing a committee-based model, we do not require the individual components to be highly optimized, complex predictors. Instead, we deliberately build our ensemble using Weak Learners.

    The Weak Learner Criterion: A hypothesis function \(h(\vec{x})\) qualifies as a weak learner if its predictive capability is strictly better than random chance.

    Let us formalize this boundary condition across different task topologies:

    • The Binary Classification Boundary (\(K=2\)): A completely random guess yields an expected accuracy of exactly \(50\%\). Therefore, any valid binary weak learner must yield an accuracy of \(50\% + \epsilon\), where \(\epsilon > 0\). If a binary model's performance falls below \(50\%\), it is mathematically broken, acting worse than a blind coin flip.
    • The Multi-Class Classification Boundary (\(K\) classes): For a \(10\)-class task, a random uniform guess yields a baseline accuracy of exactly \(10\%\). A model that achieves only \(5% \times \text{accuracy}\) is systematically performing worse than uniform random chance, introducing harmful distortion into the committee.

    As long as every individual model in our committee satisfies this weak learner criterion, ensemble theory guarantees that aggregating their predictions will systematically suppress global variance and drive down true risk.

    44.2 Bootstrap Resampling and the Out-of-Bag (OOB) Limit Proof

    The foundational challenge of ensemble learning is the acquisition of data. The variance-reduction properties of an ensemble average assume that each constituent model is trained on a completely independent dataset pulled from the environment. In live engineering scenarios, however, we do not possess a continuous reservoir of independent datasets; we have exactly one single dataset \(D\) containing \(n\) samples.

    To simulate multiple independent datasets from a single data footprint, we deploy Bootstrap Resampling with Replacement. We construct \(B\) independent synthetic training batches (\(D_1, D_2, \dots, D_B\)), each containing exactly \(n\) observations, by uniformly sampling rows from our master dataset \(D\) and returning them to the pool after each pick.

    Because samples are replaced after each draw, a single training sample can be selected multiple times within a single bootstrap batch, while other samples are passed over entirely.

    The Fundamental Out-of-Bag (OOB) Mathematical Proof

    Let us calculate the exact mathematical probability that an arbitrary training observation \(\vec{x}_i \in D\) is completely excluded from a synthetic bootstrap dataset \(D_b\) containing \(n\) samples.

    1. Because we sample uniformly with replacement across \(n\) total records, the probability of selecting our specific observation \(\vec{x}_i\) in one single draw is:
    \[P(\text{Selected}) = \frac{1}{n}\]
    1. Conversely, the probability that our observation is not chosen in that single draw is the complement:
    \[P(\text{Excluded}) = 1 - \frac{1}{n}\]
    1. To compile a full bootstrap batch \(D_b\), the system executes exactly \(n\) independent draws. Because each draw is statistically independent, the joint probability that our observation is excluded across all \(n\) sequential choices is computed via a strict product power rule:
    \[P(\text{Excluded across batch}) = \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\]

    We evaluate the mathematical limit of this expression as our dataset size scales toward infinity (\(n \to \infty\)):

    \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\]

    Recall the classical calculus definition of the exponential constant \(e^x\):

    \[\lim_{m \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{x}{m}\right)^m = e^x\]

    By matching variables and setting \(x = -1\) and \(m = n\), our limit simplifies directly into an absolute mathematical constant:

    \[\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e^{-1} = \frac{1}{e} \approx 0.367879 \quad \implies \quad \approx 36.8\%\]

    The Architectural Gift of Free Validation

    This proof establishes a foundational property of bootstrap resampling. For any given bootstrap dataset \(D_b\), approximately \(36.8\%\) of the original training samples are completely excluded from the training process. These omitted rows are formally classified as Out-of-Bag (OOB) Samples.

    This OOB threshold acts as an inherent architectural regularizer, providing an entry-free validation dataset for each individual model head in the ensemble. The \(36.8\%\) of data that tree \(b\) never saw during training can be used as a clean testing set to calculate validation error and evaluate generalization capacity on the fly, bypassing the need to sacrifice a separate, dedicated validation partition from our master dataset.

    Once these \(B\) bootstrap batches are harvested, we train \(B\) independent weak learners (\(h_1(\vec{x}), \dots, h_B(\vec{x})\)) in parallel. The final integrated model prediction—known as Bootstrap Aggregation, or Bagging—is compiled by taking a uniform average across the committee:

    \[\hat{y}_{\text{Bag}} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} h_b(\vec{x}) \quad \text{(For Continuous Regression)}\]
    \[\hat{y}_{\text{Bag}} = \text{Majority Vote}\Big( h_1(\vec{x}), \,\, \dots, \,\, h_B(\vec{x}) \Big) \quad \text{(For Discrete Classification)}\]

    43.3 Rigorous Algebraic Derivation of Correlated Ensemble Variance

    We now unroll the complete mathematical proof establishing how bootstrap aggregation affects the global variance ledger.

    Let us model the prediction output of our \(i\)-th trained weak learner on a random input coordinate as a valid dependent random variable \(Z_i \equiv h_i(\vec{x})\). Because each weak learner is trained on a bootstrap dataset pulled from the same global distribution \(P(X,Y)\), we can safely assume that these random variables are identically distributed.

    Consequently, they share an identical internal variance value, denoted as \(\sigma^2\):

    \[\text{Var}(Z_i) = \sigma^2 \quad \forall \quad i \in \{1, \,\, 2, \,\, \dots, \,\, B\}\]

    Crucially, because these models are trained on bootstrap batches derived from the same underlying dataset, they are not statistically independent. They share a positive internal correlation coefficient, denoted as \(\rho\):

    \[\text{Corr}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j) = \rho \quad \forall \quad i \neq j\]

    From probability theory, the correlation between two random variables is defined as their covariance divided by the product of their standard deviations:

    \[\rho = \frac{\text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j)}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(Z_i)}\sqrt{\text{Var}(Z_j)}} = \frac{\text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j)}{\sigma \cdot \sigma} = \frac{\text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j)}{\sigma^2}\]

    Rearranging this identity isolates the cross-model covariance as a clean product of correlation and native variance:

    \[\text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j) = \rho \sigma^2 \quad \forall \quad i \neq j\]

    We now evaluate the variance of our complete aggregated ensemble average, defined as the random variable \(\bar{Z} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} Z_i\):

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \text{Var}\left( \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} Z_i \right)\]

    We extract the scalar fraction \(\frac{1}{B}\) outside the variance operator, squaring it according to standard probability rules:

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \frac{1}{B^2} \text{Var}\left( \sum_{i=1}^{B} Z_i \right)\]

    To evaluate the variance of a sum of dependent random variables, we expand the expression into a full matrix summation over the entire covariance grid:

    \[\text{Var}\left( \sum_{i=1}^{B} Z_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^{B} \sum_{j=1}^{B} \text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j)\]

    We split this global grid summation into two distinct components: the main diagonal terms (where \(i = j\)) and the off-diagonal cross-covariance terms (where \(i \neq j\)):

    \[\sum_{i=1}^{B} \sum_{j=1}^{B} \text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j) = \sum_{i=1}^{B} \text{Var}(Z_i) + \sum_{i \neq j} \text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j)\]

    Let us calculate the exact number of terms operating within each summation:

    1. The main diagonal contains exactly \(B\) independent variance terms. Because \(\text{Var}(Z_i) = \sigma^2\), this first sum simplifies to:
    \[\sum_{i=1}^{B} \text{Var}(Z_i) = B \cdot \sigma^2\]
    1. The global covariance grid contains a total of \(B^2\) blocks. Subtracting the \(B\) diagonal slots leaves exactly \(B^2 - B = B(B-1)\) off-diagonal slots. Because every off-diagonal block shares a constant covariance of \(\rho\sigma^2\), the second sum simplifies to:
    \[\sum_{i=1}^{B} \sum_{j \neq i}^{B} \text{Cov}(Z_i, \,\, Z_j) = B(B-1)\rho\sigma^2\]

    We substitute both simplified sums back into our global ensemble equation:

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \frac{1}{B^2} \left[ B\sigma^2 + B(B-1)\rho\sigma^2 \right]\]

    We distribute the fractional scalar \(\frac{1}{B^2}\) across the brackets:

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \frac{B\sigma^2}{B^2} + \frac{B(B-1)\rho\sigma^2}{B^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{B} + \frac{B-1}{B}\rho\sigma^2\]

    We expand the second fractional term:

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{B} + \left(1 - \frac{1}{B}\right)\rho\sigma^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{B} + \rho\sigma^2 - \frac{\rho\sigma^2}{B}\]

    Finally, we group our terms by factoring out the scalar denominators to arrive at the definitive ensemble variance equation:

    \[\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{1 - \rho}{B}\sigma^2\]

    44.4 The Decorrelation Paradigm of Random Forests

    This final algebraic equation reveals a profound architectural insight. Let us analyze how the global variance reacts as we scale the size of our committee (\(B \to \infty\)):

    \[\lim_{B \to \infty} \text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \rho\sigma^2 + \lim_{B \to \infty} \left( \frac{1 - \rho}{B}\sigma^2 \right) = \rho\sigma^2 + 0 = \rho\sigma^2\]

    The second term drops out completely, but the first term (\(\rho\sigma^2\)) remains completely unaffected by the size of the committee. This isolates a hard structural bottleneck: the maximum variance reduction achieved by standard bootstrap aggregation is strictly bounded by the correlation coefficient \(\rho\) of its constituent models.

    If our weak learners are highly correlated, expanding the committee adds zero value. To break this bottleneck and drive global variance down further, we must implement an architectural constraint that explicitly decorrelates the models.

    This requirement brings us directly to The Random Forest Algorithm. A Random Forest is an advanced bagging ensemble that forces deep decorrelation across its weak learners (which are locked as decision trees) by adding an intentional node-level constraint: Random Feature Subsampling.

    When growing a standard decision tree, the optimizer scans across all \(D\) available feature dimensions at every single node to select its splitting boundaries. In a Random Forest, however, the algorithm restricts the node's vision.

    At every single node split across every tree in the ensemble, the engine randomly subsamples a highly restricted subset of \(m\) features without replacement from the global pool:

    \[m \approx \lfloor \sqrt{D} \rfloor\]

    The greedy optimizer is then forced to select its feature index \(j\) and split threshold \(\tau\) exclusively from this restricted pool. By blocking dominant, high-variance features from hijacking the split selections across all bootstrap batches, the algorithm forces individual trees to discover alternative geometric structures across the manifold. This structural restriction drives down the cross-model correlation coefficient \(\rho\), allowing the ensemble to unlock its full variance-reduction potential and minimize true risk.

    44.5 Mathematical Introduction to Boosting: Functional Optimization Across Subspaces

    While bootstrap aggregation balances variance by training independent models in parallel, Boosting adopts a fundamentally different optimization strategy. Boosting processes data sequentially and incrementally, building an additive functional chain designed to systematically minimize empirical bias.

    Let \(h_m(\vec{x})\) denote a weak learner optimized at the \(m\)-th sequential iteration of our training loop, and let \(H_m(\vec{x})\) represent the cumulative, aggregated ensemble model constructed up to that step. We formalize the core update engine of boosting through an Additive Forward Stagewise Progression Equation:

    \[H_m(\vec{x}) = H_{m-1}(\vec{x}) + \alpha_m h_m(\vec{x})\]

    Precise Asset Dimensions & Variable Verification:

    • \(H_{m-1}(\vec{x})\): The frozen, compiled ensemble state tracking all historical combinations up to step \(m-1\).
    • \(h_m(\vec{x}) \in \mathcal{H}\): The newly generated weak learner added to the chain at the active step \(m\).
    • \(\alpha_m \in \mathbb{R}\): A learnable scalar step-size coefficient tracking the explicit weight allocation assigned to the \(m\)-th model head.
    • \(H_m(\vec{x})\): The newly updated cumulative ensemble predictor.

    Notice the clear mathematical analogy here. In Chapter 44, we unrolled how standard gradient descent navigates a non-convex loss terrain by updating a parameter vector step-by-step: \(\vec{\theta}_t = \vec{\theta}_{t-1} - \alpha \vec{g}_t\).

    Boosting applies this exact same optimization logic to function spaces. Instead of moving point-by-point across a parameter space, boosting moves step-by-step across an infinite-dimensional hypothesis space.

    At each sequential iteration \(m\), the algorithm treats the existing ensemble \(H_{m-1}(\vec{x})\) as a fixed, frozen constant. It then optimizes a new functional step (\(h_m(\vec{x})\)) to act as a localized directional update, specifically targeting and correcting the structural mistakes made by the preceding layers. By scaling this new function by \(\alpha_m\) and adding it to the graph, the model systematically reduces empirical bias, transforming a chain of weak learners into an ultra-powerful global function approximator.

    Core Review Milestones

    1. The Out-of-Bag (OOB) Fraction: Running bootstrap sampling with replacement across \(n\) rows maps to an absolute exponential limit (\(\lim (1-1/n)^n = 1/e\)). This guarantees that approximately \(36.8\%\) of our training samples are excluded from each batch, providing an implicit validation dataset for free.
    2. The Correlation Penalty: The variance-reduction capacity of an ensemble average is bottlenecked by the correlation coefficient \(\rho\) between its base estimators. If individual models share identical view constraints, scaling the committee size \(B\) yields no benefit.
    3. The Functional Gradient Analog: Boosting re-frames ensemble assembly as an additive forward optimization pass over function spaces. It treats the historical ensemble state as a frozen constant and appends new weak learners sequentially to minimize empirical bias.
